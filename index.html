The 3pm–3am Report

What we know, what we don't, and what we need to find out about teenagers' discretionary hours

January 2026 · Research Synthesis

Michael Goldstein and Sean Geraghty

Something is amiss with American teenagers. More of them, on average, are languishing: not clinically depressed, but not leading purpose-filled lives either. Filling their days but not engaging.

The public conversation has become an obsession with screens. How much time on phones? Is social media causing depression? Should we ban TikTok? These are reasonable questions, but we think they may be incomplete.

This report offers a reframe: the question isn't just whether screens are harmful, but what screens are displacing. Time is zero-sum. Every hour spent scrolling is an hour not spent doing something else. And the "something else"—time with friends, part-time jobs, unstructured play, sports, sleep—may be precisely what builds the skills, relationships, and sense of purpose that young people need.

We call this the substitution hypothesis: that the path forward lies less in suppressing screen time and more in substituting something better in its place.

A note on intellectual honesty: the research on screens and mental health is more contested than popular accounts suggest. Critics like Candice Odgers at UC Irvine argue that "hundreds of researchers have searched for the kind of large effects suggested" by screen-time alarmists, producing "a mix of no, small and mixed associations."1a Tyler Cowen points to extraordinary teens using social media for scientific collaboration—"an incredible generation, smarter, more dynamic."1b We take these critiques seriously. Our argument doesn't depend on proving screens are toxic. It depends on the more modest claim that certain real-world activities are good, that those activities are declining, and that helping teens do more of them is worthwhile regardless of what we conclude about screen harms.

I. The Data Problem

Take, for example, the screen question. How much time do teenagers spend on screens, or on social media, or watching TV? It depends who you ask and what exactly they're measuring. And the averages hide enormous variation between 90th percentile and 10th percentile teenagers.

Three answers to slightly different questions
Source Methodology Answer
ATUS (BLS, 2024) "Games and computer for leisure" as primary activity 1.3 hrs1
Gallup (2023) "How much time on social media?" 4.8 hrs2
Common Sense Media (2021) Total screen entertainment (estimated) 8.4 hrs3

These numbers differ by a factor of six. Each measures something slightly different. Yet each is cited authoritatively in policy debates.

The American Time Use Survey (ATUS), conducted by the Bureau of Labor Statistics, is the gold standard for time-use research. It asks people to walk through their previous day minute by minute, recording what they were primarily doing at each moment. This methodology captures focused activity but undercounts multitasking.4 If you're scrolling your phone while watching TV, only one gets recorded.

Surveys like Gallup's ask teens to estimate their total time on social media. We suspect this overcounts: attention feels like time, and background scrolling gets lumped with focused use. (Though we haven't found rigorous research confirming this intuition.)

Neither approach is wrong. They're measuring different things. But the gap between them makes it hard to use any single headline number for policy purposes.

CTF's Working Hypothesis

The obsession with screen time hours may itself be a distraction. The more important question may be what screens are displacing, and what the cost of that displacement turns out to be.

II. What We Know Directionally

Even with messy data, certain trends are clear enough to trust. Multiple sources, using different methodologies, point in the same direction. Something has changed in how teenagers spend the 3pm–3am hours.

Time with friends: the clearest signal

ATUS data shows that Americans aged 15–19 now spend about one-third of the time socializing with friends compared to twenty years ago: a 45% decline since 2003.5 In 2024, for the first time in the survey's history, adults over 50 spent slightly more time socializing in person than teenagers did.

The Monitoring the Future survey, which has tracked high school seniors since the 1970s, tells the same story with more texture:

The decline was gradual from the 1970s through 2010: about a point every four years. Then it accelerated sharply. By 2022, 12th graders spent substantially less time on in-person social interaction than their Gen X predecessors.

"Going to the mall has gone down. Driving in the car for fun has gone down. Going to the movies has gone down. We're talking about kids who are spending five, six, seven hours a day on social media." — Jean Twenge, San Diego State University

The timing is notable. The inflection point, 2010 to 2012, coincides with smartphone adoption crossing 50% among teenagers and the rise of Instagram and Snapchat.

Other displacement effects

The decline in socializing is the clearest trend, but not the only one:

Reading: ATUS shows 15–19 year olds read for pleasure an average of 9 minutes per day. We're not certain how much of that decline reflects substitution toward other media, but pleasure reading by all accounts is down substantially. A University of Florida study found daily reading has declined 40% over twenty years, about 3% annually.7 In the late 1970s, 60% of 12th graders read a book or magazine almost daily; by 2016, that figure was 16%. High school teachers report anecdotally, in a growing body of think pieces (most recently in The Atlantic8), that students are having a harder time with sustained attention.

Sleep: The CDC Youth Risk Behavior Survey found that only 23% of high school students got the recommended 8+ hours on school nights in 2023, down from 32% in 2013.9 Evening phone use is frequently cited as a contributing factor.

Part-time work: Teen labor force participation has declined from 52% in 2000 to 37% today. Some of this reflects economic shifts, but the steepest drops came after 2010.10

Sports participation (boys): While overall youth sports rebounded post-pandemic, regular participation among boys fell from 50% in 2013 to 41% in 2023. Teen (13–17) regular participation dropped 3% even in 2024.11

The directional truth: Regardless of the exact screen time number, teenagers are spending substantially less time with friends in person, less time reading, less time sleeping, less time working, and (for boys) less time in sports than they did fifteen years ago. These are the activities being displaced. This is what we should be measuring.

III. Why Displacement Matters

The debate about teen screen time typically focuses on whether phones are harmful: whether they cause anxiety, depression, or addiction. A different question may be more important: what are teenagers losing when screens crowd out other activities?

The developmental case

Jonathan Haidt, in The Anxious Generation, argues that American childhood underwent a fundamental transformation between 2010 and 2015: from "play-based childhood" to "phone-based childhood."

His argument centers less on screens being inherently toxic than on what screens replaced:

"We've had a play-based childhood for literally 200 million years because we're mammals and all mammals play. That's how we wired up our brains. Somewhere in the 1990s, it stopped, and stopped dead by 2010. It faded away and was replaced, very suddenly, by the phone-based childhood between 2010 and 2015."

Play, especially unstructured, unsupervised play with peers, is how children have always developed social skills, risk assessment, emotional regulation, and resilience. The displacement of play by screens represents a disruption to a developmental process that mammals have relied on for hundreds of millions of years.

The labor market case

Economists have documented a dramatic shift in what the labor market rewards. Research from Harvard's David Deming shows that since 1980, jobs requiring high levels of social skills have grown substantially, while jobs requiring only cognitive skills have shrunk.12

We hypothesize that non-cognitive skills develop substantially in the 3pm–3am hours: through part-time jobs, team sports, unstructured socializing, navigating independence, managing commitments, dealing with difficult people, recovering from failures. If those hours are increasingly spent on screens, we should expect the development of non-cognitive skills to suffer, at precisely the moment when the labor market is rewarding them more than ever.

The therapeutic case

Clinical psychology offers a third line of evidence. Behavioral Activation (BA) is one of the most effective evidence-based treatments for depression. The core premise: increased engagement in meaningful activities leads to improved mood.

A 2023 meta-analysis of 22 randomized controlled trials found that individual behavioral activation produced effect sizes of g = 0.85 for depression, comparable to cognitive therapy and antidepressant medication.14 The intervention is "relatively uncomplicated, time-efficient and does not require complex skills from patients or therapists."

Related approaches in positive psychology emphasize a similar insight, sometimes summarized as "do more, feel better." The inverse is also true: decreased activity leads to worse mood, which leads to further withdrawal, creating a vicious cycle. This is precisely the pattern we see in languishing teenagers: not clinically depressed, but inactive, and the inactivity breeds low-grade malaise.

Independence Therapy

Camilo Ortiz, a clinical psychologist at Long Island University, has developed a related approach called "Independence Therapy." Working with clinically anxious children aged 9–14, he found that giving them "mega-doses of independence" (assignments to do new things on their own, without parents) reduced anxiety as effectively as traditional exposure therapy.15

As Ortiz explains: "When we do everything for them, we subtract all the uncertainty and then they suck at it. And then they're so anxious because you put them in any situation that has an element of uncertainty and they fall apart."

One patient, a 9-year-old girl afraid to sleep in her own bed, was given assignments to take public transit alone, buy items at the store, and navigate small setbacks. During the final week of treatment, unprompted, she began sleeping in her own bed, something she had never done before.

"Just as anxiety becomes generalized and everything becomes too dangerous and too scary, competence and confidence also generalize. Children's competence gives them confidence, and then the parents get it too, and they're spiraling up instead of spiraling down." — Lenore Skenazy, Let Grow

This is the therapeutic parallel to the developmental and economic arguments: real-world activity builds skills, confidence, and wellbeing. Screens, whatever their direct effects, displace the activities that produce these benefits.

IV. The Substitution Hypothesis

If displacement is the problem, the solution may lie less in reducing screen time directly than in substituting something better in its place.

Why suppression alone may not be enough

Efforts to restrict teen screen use have shown limited success outside of school. China has tried since 2019 with various restrictions, including video game time limits; a Nature Human Behaviour study found "no credible evidence" the tactics reduced gaming.16 Britain's Online Safety Act took effect in July 2025; VPN signups surged by 1,400% within hours.17 Parental controls (screen time limits, content filters) are widely available but rarely effective. The dynamic of a parent peering over a teenager's shoulder asking "Is that really homework?" is difficult to sustain.

School phone bans during the 8am–3pm hours have strong logic and growing evidence behind them. But from 3pm to 3am, when teens have more autonomy and parents have less control, suppression faces structural limits.

The broken boredom mechanism

There's a deeper reason suppression alone may be insufficient. Once upon a time, boredom nudged teenagers to do things. The discomfort of having nothing to do propelled them into the world: to call a friend, ride a bike, get a job, find trouble, discover something.

Teenagers with screens have largely eliminated that discomfort. They never have to sit with boredom for more than a few seconds. This removes the nudge mechanism that once pushed young people toward real-world activity.

Substitution works inherently. If you're at soccer practice, you're off your phone. If you're working a restaurant shift, you're engaged with real people. The goal is to make real life more compelling, so that it crowds out passive screen time naturally.

Towards a vision of flourishing

Flourishing is a more holistic concept than happiness. Researchers like Corey Keyes, Tyler VanderWeele at Harvard, and Carol Ryff have done important work conceptualizing what it means.18 The field is still working out precise definitions. But flourishing means something deeper than momentary contentment: it combines hedonic wellbeing (feeling good) with eudaimonic wellbeing (functioning well, having purpose, being engaged).

We find a gap between the academic definitions of flourishing and what we observe in real time. The academic measures capture a state. What we see are the activities that appear to lead to that state: the hypothesized drivers. Our working theory is that engaging more deeply in how you spend your time, and trying to align it toward activities that build a flourishing life, produces better outcomes. Both the feeling good and the functioning well, together.

Flourishing teenagers share a common pattern: busy calendars. Their out-of-school hours are filled with a portfolio of activities across multiple domains:

The Portfolio of a Flourishing Teen
  • Physical: A sport, gym routine, dance class, outdoor activity
  • Social: Regular in-person time with friends
  • Economic: A part-time job (up to 10–15 hours/week)
  • Creative: Music, art, building, coding: making rather than consuming
  • Community: Youth group, scouting, volunteering, civic engagement
  • Rest: Adequate sleep, 7–8 hours most nights
Thursday in the Life of a Flourishing Teen

Maya, 16, gets home from school at 3:15. She has a snack, scrolls her phone for twenty minutes, then changes for her 4:30 shift at the ice cream shop downtown. She works until 8, chatting with coworkers between customers, dealing with a mildly difficult parent who complains about portion sizes. Her manager, a college student, shows her how to close out the register.

After her mom picks her up, she eats dinner, does about an hour of homework, and texts with friends about weekend plans. She's in bed by 10:30, tired but not depleted. She didn't do anything extraordinary today. But her hours were filled with real people, small challenges, and genuine engagement with the world outside her phone.

Some teenagers build this portfolio through school activities: sports teams, theater, band, clubs. Others assemble it independently through community organizations, jobs, and informal networks. Both paths work. What matters is the result: a life that crowds out passive screen time by being genuinely engaging.

The equity problem

But here we must be careful. The "flourishing activities" we've described are not equally accessible. The Aspen Institute's State of Play reports document a growing participation gap: in 2012, children from homes earning under $25,000 played sports at a rate 13.6 percentage points lower than those from homes earning $100,000+. By 2024, that gap had widened to 20.2 points.17a Kids from the lowest-income homes now play at half the rate of their wealthiest peers.

Youth sports costs have risen 46% since 2019. Black youth participation has dropped from 45% in 2013 to 35% in 2023. The "portfolio of flourishing" we describe may be a portrait of privilege as much as a prescription for wellbeing.

This complicates the substitution hypothesis. If the alternative activities are themselves stratified by income and race, then "substitute something better" risks becoming advice that only works for families who can afford club soccer, music lessons, and summer camps. Any serious agenda must grapple with access: free community sports leagues, subsidized after-school programs, part-time job opportunities in all neighborhoods.

The temperament question

There's another complication. The flourishing teen we've described—busy calendar, team sports, part-time job, lots of socializing—may reflect one temperament more than others. What about introverts? What about teens whose flourishing looks like deep reading, solitary creative work, or one-on-one friendships rather than group activities?

We don't think the answer is that introverts should become extroverts. But we do think even introverted flourishing requires some real-world activity: the deep reader still needs sleep, the solitary artist still benefits from occasional collaboration, the one-on-one friend still needs that one friend to be physically present sometimes. The concern isn't that all teens need identical portfolios. It's that passive consumption—scrolling without creating, watching without doing—doesn't serve anyone's temperament well.

V. What We Need

If this analysis is right, the research and policy agenda should shift. We need less attention to screen time itself and more attention to what screens displace.

1. Better measurement of what kids are actually doing

The current measurement landscape is backwards. We have multiple estimates of screen hours (even if they don't agree) but almost no systematic data on the activities that may matter most for flourishing:

Activity Data Quality Notes
Screen time Multiple sources Conflicting methodologies
Time with friends (in person) ATUS, MTF Declining, per diary data
Part-time work BLS, moderate Declining participation
Sports participation Aspen, moderate Complex picture
Music/arts practice Very poor No systematic tracking
Time outdoors Very poor Almost no data
Unstructured play Very poor No national measure
Volunteering Moderate ATUS tracks; low rates

What gets measured reflects what we're anxious about, not necessarily what matters. We need time-use instruments designed to capture flourishing activities with the same rigor we bring to screen time.

2. An "hours toward flourishing" framework

Instead of measuring harm, we should measure the positive. We propose developing a framework for tracking hours that could lead to a state of flourishing per week: time spent on activities the research suggests actually build wellbeing and capability.

A working definition might include:

  • Physical fitness and sports
  • In-person time with friends
  • Adequate sleep
  • Work or volunteering (up to 10–15 hours)
  • Pleasure reading
  • Creative activity, outdoor time, community participation

We acknowledge this is extremely messy. What counts as "flourishing" involves value judgments, and the boundaries between categories are often fuzzy. The measurement challenges are real, and reasonable people will disagree about what belongs on the list. But we think the work of figuring it out is worth doing, because without a positive metric, we're stuck measuring what we're against rather than what we're for.

3. Understanding the economics of substitution

The critical unanswered question: what happens if we substitute flourishing activities back in?

Haidt argues that phones have displaced play, and makes a series of developmental arguments for why that matters. Clinical research on behavioral activation and independence therapy suggests that increasing real-world activity produces measurable benefits. Labor economists have documented rising returns to non-cognitive skills.

But we don't have rigorous field experiments that actually substitute activities for screen time and measure the results. We need R&D on:

  • Which activities produce any wellbeing and developmental gains?
  • What's the dose-response relationship between flourishing activities and outcomes?
  • Do the benefits vary by demographic group, family structure, or baseline characteristics?

We hypothesize we know what a flourishing teen's calendar looks like. We don't know enough about how to help languishing teens build one.

VI. From Languishing to Flourishing

A suburban Boston-area teacher recently described what he called his median student:

Portrait of the Languishing Teen
  • Heavy screen time after school, over the weekend, and during the summer
  • Little time outdoors, reading books, walking, engaged in fitness, working or volunteering
  • Apathy; maybe low-grade anxiety or depression (unclear if clinical)
  • Going through the motions, little direction or ambition
  • No clear out-of-school "passions"
  • Hasn't found "their people"

This portrait, recognizable to teachers, parents, and teenagers themselves, captures something that doesn't fit neatly into clinical categories. These kids aren't depressed enough for diagnosis. They're not in crisis. They're just... languishing.

As Yuval Levin wrote: "The challenges to America's social order now seem less like exorbitant human desires driving people's lives out of control and more like an absence of energy and drive leaving people languishing and enervated."19

The screen time debate has been dominated by questions of harm. But harm may be the wrong frame. The question isn't whether phones are bad for teenagers. The question is whether the life that remains after phones take their share is good enough to produce flourishing.

The therapeutic research is clear: activity produces wellbeing. The developmental research is clear: play builds capability. The economic research is clear: non-cognitive skills have never been more valuable. And the time-use data, for all its messiness, points clearly in one direction: the activities that build these things are in decline.

We don't need to prove that screens are harmful. We need to prove that the alternative is better, and then help teenagers actually live it.
