Center for Teen Flourishing

The 3pm to 3am Report

What we know about teenagers' discretionary hours, and what we're trying to find out

January 2026 · Research Synthesis

Michael Goldstein and Sean Geraghty

This document provides the evidence base for CTF's research agenda. For a summary of what we're building and what we're asking for, see the accompanying pitch memo.

"Less screen time" is not a vision. Everyone agrees something has gone wrong with American teenagers, but telling kids to put down their phones doesn't answer the question of what they should do instead.

This report examines what teenagers do from 3pm to 3am: the hours when school ends and autonomy begins. What happens in these hours shapes wellbeing and development. The evidence that something has changed is robust, even as the causal explanations remain contested.

The public conversation has focused on screens: how much time on phones, whether social media causes depression, whether to ban TikTok. We think this framing, while understandable, misses the more important point. Whether or not screens are directly harmful (the experimental evidence is surprisingly thin), the opportunity cost is enormous. Every hour spent scrolling is an hour not spent with friends, not spent reading, not spent working a part-time job, not spent playing sports, not spent sleeping. The displacement is real regardless of whether the thing doing the displacing is itself toxic.

What's missing is a positive account of teenage flourishing, and a research agenda oriented toward producing it.

I. Action Precedes Mood

The conventional wisdom about motivation: first you feel motivated, then you act. You wait until you're "in the mood" to exercise, study, or see friends. Motivation is the spark; action follows.

The clinical evidence suggests this is backwards. And this reversal is the conceptual foundation for everything CTF does.

Behavioral Activation (BA) is one of the most effective treatments for depression. Developed in the 1970s and refined since, it rests on a simple insight: depressed people withdraw from activities, and the withdrawal makes the depression worse. The treatment reverses this by systematically scheduling activities and tracking their effects on mood. You do not wait to feel better before acting. You act, and feeling better follows.1

A 2023 meta-analysis of 22 randomized controlled trials found that behavioral activation produced effect sizes comparable to cognitive therapy and antidepressant medication.2 The approach works by changing what people do, not how they think.

The mechanism is straightforward. Activity generates positive reinforcement: mastery experiences, social connection, pleasure, meaning. Inactivity removes these reinforcers and generates its own negative spiral. The less you do, the worse you feel; the worse you feel, the less you do. Breaking the cycle requires acting before the motivation arrives.

"Motivation is not a prerequisite for action. It is a consequence of it." — Christopher Martell, Behavioral Activation for Depression

This has direct implications for languishing teenagers. The languishing teen is not clinically depressed, but shows a similar pattern: withdrawal from activities, low-grade malaise, waiting to feel like doing something before doing it. The wait can last a long time. The intervention that works for depression (scheduling activity, acting before the mood arrives) may work for languishing too.

Recent evidence supports the transfer. The Harvard Mental Health Data for All Lab has tested Behavioral Activation in Texas community settings with lightly trained staff, finding effectiveness with subclinical populations. The active ingredient appears to be the structure itself: mapping current behavior, identifying barriers, scheduling change, and building accountability. If BA works with non-specialists delivering it to people who aren't clinically depressed, the path to adolescents is shorter than it might seem.

Camilo Ortiz, a clinical psychologist at Long Island University, has tested a related approach with anxious children. His "Independence Therapy" gives kids assignments to do new things on their own, without parents. The result: reduced anxiety comparable to traditional exposure therapy. "When we do everything for them," Ortiz explains, "we subtract all the uncertainty and then they suck at it."3 Ortiz's work is closer to the languishing teen population than clinical BA: these are kids who aren't diagnosably ill but who have withdrawn from the experiences through which competence develops.

The common thread: competence and confidence come from doing, not from feeling ready to do. This is what the 3pm to 3am hours are for—engagement with the world in ways that build the resources for flourishing. The question is whether those hours are being used that way.

From 2013 to 2021, the share of high school students reporting persistent sadness or hopelessness rose from 30% to 42%. The 2023 data showed a slight improvement, down to 40%, the first decline in a decade.4 We show this not because it is novel but because we want to ask a different question about it. The public conversation has become skilled at describing what is going wrong with teenagers. It has been less interested in articulating what going right would actually look like.

Corey Keyes, a sociologist at Emory, noticed in the late 1990s that psychology had developed sophisticated instruments for measuring depression, anxiety, and dysfunction, but almost nothing for measuring their opposites. In a 2002 paper that shaped much of the subsequent research, he proposed a "mental health continuum" running from languishing to flourishing.5 Languishing is not depression. It is emptiness, stagnation, the quiet desperation of going through the motions. Flourishing involves both feeling good and functioning well: positive emotions, engagement with challenging activities, a sense of meaning, close relationships.

What made the framework consequential was Keyes's demonstration that flourishing and mental illness are not simply opposites on a single scale. You can be depressed and still have moments of meaning. You can be free of any diagnosable condition and still be languishing. In his data, only about 17% of American adults were flourishing; the majority were "moderately mentally healthy" but not thriving.6 The implication for policy: reducing illness is not the same as promoting health.

The predictive power of languishing is striking. In longitudinal follow-up, Keyes found that adults who were languishing in 1995 were six times more likely to develop major depression by 2005 than those who were flourishing. Languishing is not a stable resting state. It is a risk factor.

Flourishing, though, is typically measured as a state. You survey people and ask how they feel. What interests us more are the leading indicators of that state: the activities and behaviors that predict whether someone will flourish. Barbara Fredrickson's "broaden and build" theory offers a framework.7 Positive emotions expand cognitive and behavioral repertoires. Joy makes us want to play; interest makes us want to explore; contentment makes us want to savor and integrate. Over time, these broadened mindsets build durable resources: skills, relationships, resilience. The activities that generate positive emotions are not luxuries. They are the mechanisms through which psychological resources accumulate.

This reframes the question. Instead of asking "how sad are teenagers?" we can ask "how are teenagers spending their time, and does that time-use predict flourishing?" The 3pm to 3am hours are when discretionary activity happens. When teenagers spend less time with friends, they build fewer of the relationships that Keyes identifies as central to mental health. When they spend less time in challenging pursuits, they miss the mastery experiences that generate confidence. When they spend less time in unstructured play, they lose the experimentation through which identity forms. The hours matter because of what happens in them.

III. The State of the Data

Anyone who spends time with the research on teen wellbeing will eventually notice that the landscape is harder to navigate than it first appears. This is less a criticism of the researchers involved than a description of what happens when multiple surveys, designed for different purposes, attempt to measure overlapping phenomena using incompatible methods.

The measurement problem

Different surveys produce different numbers for the same phenomenon. How much time do teenagers spend on screens? It depends who you ask.

Three surveys, three different answers
Source Methodology Teen screen time
ATUS (BLS, 2024) Time diary: respondents walk through previous day minute by minute, recording primary activity 1.3 hrs/day8
Gallup (2023) Self-report estimate: "How many hours per day do you spend on social media?" 4.8 hrs/day9
Common Sense (2021) Expanded definition: all screen-based entertainment including TV, gaming, and background media 8.4 hrs/day10

These numbers differ by a factor of six. Each measures something slightly different. Each is cited authoritatively in policy debates. When a parent encounters a headline number for "teen screen time," they have no way to evaluate which source it came from, what the number actually means, or how much variation it obscures.

The American Time Use Survey (ATUS) is the gold standard for time-use research: respondents walk through their previous day minute by minute, recording their primary activity. This captures focused attention but misses multitasking. If you scroll your phone while watching TV, only one gets recorded. Self-report surveys like Gallup's ask for estimates, which tend to run high—people are notoriously bad at estimating their own behavior. Common Sense uses an expansive definition that includes background media. The gap between these approaches makes it difficult to anchor policy discussions in any single number.

The causation problem

When researchers run experiments asking people to reduce social media use, they typically find improvements in wellbeing. The effects are real but modest.

A 0.05 SD improvement is roughly equivalent to moving from the 50th to the 52nd percentile.11

Candice Odgers, a developmental psychologist at UC Irvine, reviewed this literature in Nature and concluded that researchers have searched for large effects and found "a mix of no, small and mixed associations."12 Matthew Gentzkow, an economist at Stanford and one of the authors of the 2020 Facebook deactivation study, offered a more nuanced view: "The really convincing causal evidence we have is quite limited. But if you put all that together, I think it's enough to say there is a substantial probability that these harms are large and real."13

We take a different view. The experimental evidence for direct harm from screens is thin enough that reasonable people can disagree. But the practical question may not depend on resolving this debate. If teenagers are spending less time on activities that build flourishing—and the evidence for that is robust—then helping them re-engage with those activities could improve their lives regardless of what caused the disengagement in the first place.

IV. What We Know

Certain trends are clear despite these measurement challenges. When multiple sources using different methodologies point in the same direction, that is signal.

Time with friends

ATUS data shows that Americans aged 15 to 19 now spend about one-third as much time socializing with friends in person as they did twenty years ago.14 In 2024, adults over 50 spent slightly more time socializing in person than teenagers did.

The Monitoring the Future survey, run by the University of Michigan since 1976, tells the same story:

The decline was gradual from the 1970s through 2010, then accelerated. The inflection point coincides with smartphone adoption crossing 50% among teenagers. Correlation is not causation, but the timing is suggestive.

Other displacements

Reading: 15- to 19-year-olds read for pleasure an average of 9 minutes per day. Daily reading has declined 40% over twenty years.16

Sleep: Only 23% of high school students got 8+ hours on school nights in 2023, down from 32% in 2013.17

Part-time work: Teen labor force participation has declined from 52% in 2000 to 37% today.18

Sports (boys): Regular participation fell from 50% in 2013 to 41% in 2023.19

CTF's hypothesis: Regardless of the exact screen time number, teenagers are spending less time in the activities through which flourishing develops: relationships, challenging pursuits, rest, work, play. This displacement is what matters.

V. Why It Matters

Flourishing for its own sake

Most of the public conversation about teen wellbeing is framed instrumentally. We worry about mental health because depression hurts academic performance. We worry about social skills because the labor market rewards them. This framing is not wrong, but it is incomplete.

Tyler VanderWeele, a Harvard epidemiologist, has proposed a framework for human flourishing that treats it as an end in itself, not merely a means to other outcomes.20 His model identifies five domains, each of which is "nearly universally desired" and "constitutes an end in and of itself":

Notice what this framework does not include: income, career success, academic achievement. These may contribute to flourishing, but they are not themselves what flourishing consists of. A teenager who has close friendships, a sense of purpose, good health, moments of happiness, and is developing their character is flourishing, regardless of their GPA or their future earning potential.

The 3pm to 3am hours matter because they are when the building blocks of flourishing get constructed. Time with friends builds relationships. Challenging activities build character and generate meaning. Physical activity supports health. The displacement of these hours by passive scrolling is a problem not because it hurts test scores or labor market outcomes (though it may), but because it deprives young people of the experiences through which a good life is built.

The labor market

Economists have documented a shift in what the labor market rewards. Research from Harvard's David Deming shows that since 1980, jobs requiring high levels of social interaction grew by nearly 12 percentage points as a share of the U.S. labor force, while math-intensive but low-social jobs shrank by 3.3 percentage points.21

In experimental work, Deming and Ben Weidmann found that social skills improve team performance about as much as IQ, and that "team players" who consistently help groups exceed expectations score significantly higher on measures of social intelligence.22 Job posting data confirms the pattern: firms that demand both cognitive and social skills pay higher wages and outperform competitors, evidence of what Deming calls "cognitive social-skill complementarity."23

Social skills develop in the 3pm to 3am hours: through part-time jobs, team sports, unstructured socializing, navigating independence, managing commitments, dealing with difficult people, recovering from small failures. If those hours are increasingly spent in isolation, these skills suffer at precisely the moment when the labor market rewards them most.

Access and activation

The activities through which flourishing develops are not equally accessible. The Aspen Institute reports a growing participation gap in youth sports: children from homes earning under $25,000 now play at half the rate of their wealthiest peers. Youth sports costs have risen 46% since 2019.24

But access is not the whole story. Affluent teenagers are also languishing. They have money for activities, parents with bandwidth, transportation, and many are still on their phones for hours, still withdrawing from engagement, still going through the motions. For these teens, the barrier is not resources. It is activation energy: the executive function load of finding something to do, committing to it, showing up, and sustaining participation over time.

This suggests that languishing may have (at least) two distinct sources. For some teens, the constraint is structural: they would engage if activities were affordable and accessible. For others, the constraint is motivational: the activities exist, but the activation energy required to start exceeds what they can summon on their own. These are different problems requiring different interventions. A subsidy helps the first group but not the second. A coach providing scaffolding and accountability might help the second group but does nothing for the first.

CTF's focus on activation coaching is a bet that the second problem is more widespread than commonly assumed, and more tractable than it appears. We may be wrong. That is why we are running a study with a stipend-only arm: if money alone moves teens as much as coaching does, we will know that friction was not the binding constraint after all.

The broader point is this: the displacement of the 3pm to 3am hours is real, the consequences are serious, and the path back to flourishing will not be the same for every teenager. What we need now is not another survey documenting the problem, but rigorous tests of what actually helps. That is what CTF intends to provide.
