The 3pm–3am Report

How American teenagers actually spend their discretionary hours—and what we still don't know

January 2026 · Research Synthesis

Every day, between 3pm and 3am, American teenagers make thousands of small decisions about how to spend their time. These twelve hours—after school ends and before the next day begins—represent the window of greatest autonomy in adolescent life. What happens in this window matters enormously for development, wellbeing, and flourishing.

Yet a significant share of American teenagers are not flourishing in these hours. They are languishing—not clinically depressed, but not thriving either. A suburban Boston-area teacher recently described his median student this way:

Portrait of the Languishing Teen
  • Heavy screen time after school, over the weekend, and during the summer
  • Little time outdoors, reading books, walking, engaged in fitness, working or volunteering
  • Apathy; maybe low-grade anxiety or depression (unclear if clinical)
  • Going through the motions, little direction or ambition
  • No clear out-of-school "passions"
  • Hasn't found "their people"

This portrait—recognizable to teachers, parents, and teenagers themselves—sits at the center of a growing concern. As Yuval Levin wrote in a recent essay, "the challenges to America's social order now seem less like exorbitant human desires driving people's lives out of control and more like an absence of energy and drive leaving people languishing and enervated."1

This report synthesizes what we actually know about how teenagers spend the 3pm–3am hours—and names what we don't. Our aim is not to document harm, but to understand the preconditions for flourishing.

I. The Complete Picture: A Teenager's Day

The American Time Use Survey (ATUS) is the gold standard for understanding how people spend their time. Conducted continuously since 2003 by the Bureau of Labor Statistics, it asks roughly 7,700 Americans each year to account for every minute of a single day. Unlike surveys that ask people to estimate their weekly habits, ATUS captures actual behavior through detailed time diaries.

Here, for the first time in a synthesis designed for general audiences, is how Americans aged 15–19 spend a typical day:2

Television watching—at 2 hours per day—remains the largest single leisure activity for teenagers, larger than gaming, social media, or any other category ATUS measures. "Screen time" is not synonymous with "phone time."

II. Digital Life: What Multiple Sources Tell Us

ATUS provides the most rigorous time-use data available, but its categories were designed for an earlier era. "Games and computer use for leisure" captures something, but not everything, about digital life. To understand how teenagers engage with technology, we must triangulate across multiple sources.

A methodological note

Why the numbers don't match

ATUS shows 1h 19m of "games and computer use for leisure." Gallup shows 4.8 hours of social media. How can both be true?

The answer lies in methodology. ATUS asks about primary activities—what you were mainly doing at each moment. Gallup asks teens to estimate total time on social media, which may include background use while doing other things (eating, watching TV, even in class). Neither number is "wrong," but they measure different things. ATUS likely undercounts passive/background phone use; self-report surveys likely overcount because attention feels like time.

The truth is probably somewhere between: more than the diary captures, less than teens perceive.

Demographic disparities

Digital engagement varies significantly by race and ethnicity. Pew finds:

Group Online "almost constantly" TikTok "almost constantly"
Hispanic teens 58% 25%
Black teens 53% 28%
White teens 37% 8%

Source: Pew Research Center, December 2024

These gaps—often exceeding 20 percentage points—are larger than the increases in screen time that generate headlines. We don't yet understand whether this reflects different access patterns, cultural factors, or measurement artifacts. What we do know is that any analysis of "teens" as a monolith misses crucial variation.

III. Beyond Screens: The Activities We're Losing

The debate about teen screen time often centers on whether digital media is harmful. A different question may be more important: what are screens displacing? The evidence suggests several forms of activity have declined precipitously—and that a critical mechanism has broken.

The broken boredom mechanism: Once upon a time, boredom nudged teens to do things. The smartphone and the trillion-dollar companies who profit from them have defeated boredom. That leads to an idleness that may be deeply harmful to young people—not because phones are inherently toxic, but because they've eliminated the discomfort that once prompted action.

Time with friends: A collapse

Perhaps no trend is more striking than the decline in face-to-face socializing. ATUS data shows Americans aged 15–19 now spend one-third of the time socializing with friends compared to 20 years ago.3

The Monitoring the Future survey, which has tracked high school seniors since the 1970s, shows the trajectory in detail:

Year % who meet friends "almost daily"
Late 1970s ~52%
2010 44%

Source: Monitoring the Future, University of Michigan

The decline was gradual from the 1970s to 2010, then accelerated sharply—coinciding precisely with smartphone adoption. By 2017, 12th-graders spent an hour less on in-person social interaction per day than their Gen X predecessors.

"Going to the mall has gone down. Driving in the car for fun has gone down. Going to the movies has gone down. We're talking about kids who are spending five, six, seven hours a day on social media." — Jean Twenge, San Diego State University

Critically, this decline in socializing has coincided with a rise in loneliness. Among 12th graders, 39% said they "often felt lonely" in 2017, up from 26% in 2012—an all-time high since the question was first asked in 1977.4

Reading: Near extinction

ATUS shows that 15–19 year olds read for pleasure an average of 9 minutes per day. This is not a typo. Nine minutes.

A comprehensive study from the University of Florida and University College London, analyzing 20 years of ATUS data, found:

Key Finding

Daily reading for pleasure in the United States has declined by more than 40% over the last 20 years—"a sustained, steady decline of about 3% per year."

The National Assessment of Educational Progress confirms the trajectory for young people specifically:

Measure 2012 2023 Change
13-year-olds reading daily for fun 27% 14% −13 pts
Average reading score (13-year-olds) 263 256 −7 pts

Source: National Endowment for the Arts / NAEP

In the late 1970s, 60% of 12th-graders read a book or magazine almost daily. By 2016, that figure was 16%.5

Sports participation: A complicated picture

Unlike reading and socializing, sports participation tells a more nuanced story. The Aspen Institute's State of Play 2025 report finds:

  • Overall participation is rebounding: 65% of youth ages 6–17 played a sport at least once in 2024—the highest since tracking began in 2012.
  • But boys are playing less: Regular sports participation among boys dropped from 50% in 2013 to 41% in 2023.
  • And teen participation is declining: Regular participation for ages 13–17 fell 3% in 2024, even as younger children increased.
  • Income gaps are widening: The participation gap between highest and lowest income households grew from 13.6 points in 2012 to 20.2 points in 2024.

ATUS confirms that 15–19 year olds average 38 minutes per day on sports and exercise—but this is an average across all teens, including the majority who do zero on any given day. The distribution matters: some teens are highly active; many are not active at all.

What we can't measure

The activities listed above—socializing, reading, sports—are at least tracked by major surveys. Other activities we might consider important for flourishing are barely measured at all:

Activity Data Quality Notes
Music lessons/practice Poor No national systematic tracking
Arts participation Poor Sporadic surveys only
Religious/spiritual activity Moderate ATUS tracks; declining
Volunteering Moderate ATUS tracks; low for teens
Time in nature Very poor Almost no systematic data
Unstructured outdoor play Very poor No national measure exists

The measurement asymmetry: We can tell you that teens average 4.8 hours per day on social media. We cannot tell you how many hours they spend outdoors, making art, playing music, or engaging in unstructured play. The activities we measure reflect our anxieties, not necessarily what matters most for development.

IV. Two Theories of Change

Social psychologist Jonathan Haidt has offered the most influential framework for understanding teen screen time. In The Anxious Generation (2024), he argues that American childhood underwent a fundamental transformation between 2010 and 2015—a shift from "play-based childhood" to "phone-based childhood."

"We've had a play-based childhood for literally 200 million years because we're mammals and all mammals play. That's how we wired up our brains. Somewhere in the 1990s, it stopped—and stopped dead by 2010. It faded away and was replaced, very suddenly, by the phone-based childhood between 2010 and 2015."

Haidt identifies four mechanisms by which phone-based childhood harms development: social deprivation, sleep deprivation, attention fragmentation, and addiction. His policy recommendations center on suppression: no smartphones before high school, no social media before 16, phone-free schools.

The school-level recommendations have gained traction. Many districts now ban phones during class. But outside of school—in the 3pm–3am window—is suppression working?

The limits of suppression

Early evidence suggests teens find workarounds:

  • China has tried since 2019 with various restrictions, including video game time limits. A Nature Human Behaviour paper found "no credible evidence" the tactics reduced gaming.6
  • Britain's Online Safety Act took effect in late 2024; VPN downloads multiplied by twenty times.
  • Parental controls offered by Big Tech—screen time limits, content filters—are widely available but rarely effective. The dynamic of a parent peering over a teenager's shoulder asking "Is that really homework?" is not a winning strategy.

None of this means suppression is worthless. School phone bans during the 8am–3pm hours have strong logic and growing evidence behind them. But from 3pm to 3am, when teens have more autonomy and parents have less control, suppression faces structural limits.

The case for substitution

An alternative theory focuses not on reducing screen time directly, but on filling teens' lives with more compelling real-world alternatives. The logic:

Core Insight

Substitution's advantage is that it inherently works. If you're rock climbing, you're probably off your phone. If you're at Starbucks with friends, you're engaged in face-to-face interaction. The goal is not to make screens less attractive, but to make real life more attractive.

This doesn't mean substitution is easy. Because screens have defeated boredom, languishing teens feel no discomfort that would push them toward activity. Traditional mentorship programs—"Hey, want to go to a ball game?"—don't work well with this population. They're not bored, so anyone trying to engage them has to be exceptionally good at motivation and persistence.

But substitution offers something suppression doesn't: a positive vision of what the 3pm–3am hours could look like.

V. What Flourishing Looks Like

If we shift from preventing harm to promoting flourishing, we need a different framework—and different metrics. What does a flourishing teen's 3pm–3am window actually contain?

Flourishing teens build busy calendars

Observationally, teenagers who are thriving share a common pattern: their out-of-school hours are filled with activities. Not random activities, but a portfolio that spans multiple domains:

The Portfolio of a Flourishing Teen
  • Physical: A sport, gym routine, dance class, or outdoor activity
  • Social: Regular in-person time with friends—Starbucks hangouts, pickup games, shared hobbies
  • Economic: A part-time job—restaurant, retail, babysitting—up to 10–15 hours/week
  • Creative: Music, art, writing, building, coding—something they make rather than consume
  • Community: Church youth group, scouting, volunteering, civic engagement
  • Rest: Adequate sleep—at least 7–8 hours most nights

Each of these domains has its own research literature showing benefits. But the key insight is that flourishing isn't about optimizing any single activity—it's about the combination. A busy calendar crowds out passive screen time by making real life more engaging.

Some flourishing teens build this portfolio through school-based activities: sports teams, theater, band, clubs. Others assemble it independently through community organizations, jobs, and informal social networks. Both paths work—what matters is the result.

A new metric: Hours of flourishing

If languishing is the problem, we need a metric that captures its opposite. We propose: hours of flourishing per week.

This metric counts weekly hours spent on:

  • Physical fitness and sports
  • In-person time with friends
  • Sleep above 5 hours per night
  • Work or volunteering (up to 10–15 hours)
  • Pleasure reading
  • Creative activities, outdoor time, community participation

Based on available data and field observation, we estimate the current distribution:

~20 hrs
75th percentile teen
~10 hrs
Median teen
~2 hrs
25th percentile teen

A teen at the 75th percentile might have: an after-school sport (6 hrs), a weekend job (8 hrs), some hangout time with friends (3 hrs), and a hobby (3 hrs). A teen at the 25th percentile might have: almost nothing—perhaps occasional socializing and sporadic exercise, but no consistent activities filling their calendar.

The gap between 20 hours and 2 hours represents vastly different developmental trajectories—and vastly different qualities of life during adolescence itself.

VI. What We Still Don't Know

Synthesizing all available data reveals not just what we know, but the contours of what we don't. Addressing teen languishing will require new research in several areas.

The syndrome problem

Teen languishing resembles what pediatricians call "failure to thrive" (FTT) in infants: not a single-organ disease but a syndrome with interacting causes. FTT involves poor weight gain, lethargy, and sleep trouble—but the underlying factors span feeding routines, parental capacity, reflux, infections, and stress. The fix is never just "see GI" or "see psych." It requires coordinated intervention across domains.

Teen languishing works similarly. The research silos—mental health, parenting, K–12 education, youth development, screen addiction, workforce development—each see one piece. But the phenomenon crosses all of them, unified only by the daily schedule: how teens actually spend the 3pm–3am hours.

We don't yet have the integrated research infrastructure to study the syndrome as a syndrome.

Three urgent questions

1. What is the actual distribution?

Most data reports averages. But averages obscure distribution. Common Sense Media found that 41% of teens spent more than 8 hours per day on screens in 2021, up from 29% in 2019. This suggests a bimodal distribution: some teens use screens moderately; others are immersed almost entirely. We don't know enough about who falls into each group, or what distinguishes them.

2. What is actually happening during screen time?

We measure duration but not content or context. An hour on YouTube could mean watching educational content, following a makeup tutorial, viewing violent content, or passively scrolling algorithmic recommendations. An hour on social media could mean messaging close friends, comparing oneself to influencers, encountering harassment, or creating content. Time-based metrics tell us almost nothing about experience.

3. How do we increase participation in flourishing activities?

We have extensive research on what activities benefit teens. We have almost no rigorous field experiments on how to get languishing teens to actually do them. The challenge is harder than it sounds: because screens have eliminated boredom, traditional motivational approaches don't work. We need R&D on engagement strategies—and honest measurement of what works.

A research agenda

Four Research Priorities

1. Comprehensive time-use synthesis: Extract and publish teen-specific ATUS data annually, paired with methodological work to better capture digital activity within the diary framework.

2. Positive activity measurement: Develop and standardize tracking of time outdoors, in creative activity, in unstructured play, and with friends—the activities that may matter most for flourishing.

3. Hours-of-flourishing baseline: Establish population-level estimates of the distribution of flourishing hours, with demographic breakdowns, to enable progress tracking.

4. Intervention R&D: Fund and rigorously evaluate programs that aim to increase beneficial activities—not just decrease screen time—measuring both time-use and wellbeing outcomes.

VII. From Languishing to Flourishing

The current conversation about teenagers and screens is stuck. One side emphasizes harms and calls for restrictions; the other notes weak correlations and counsels caution. Neither side offers a compelling positive vision of what adolescent life should look like.

This report has tried to shift the frame. The question is not only "how do we reduce screen time?" but "how do we help teenagers build lives worth living from 3pm to 3am?"

The answer won't come from any single intervention. It will require:

  • Better data on how teens actually spend their time, including the activities we want to promote
  • Honest acknowledgment of what we don't know, including the limits of suppression-based approaches
  • Field experimentation with substitution strategies that can compete with the pull of the screen
  • Cross-silo collaboration that treats languishing as the syndrome it is
  • A new metric—hours of flourishing—that measures progress toward a positive goal

We have built a research infrastructure around documenting harm. We have not built an equivalent infrastructure for understanding—and promoting—flourishing. That is the work ahead.
