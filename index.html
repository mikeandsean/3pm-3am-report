The 3pm to 3am Report

What we know, what we don't, and what we need to find out about teenagers' discretionary hours

January 2026 · Research Synthesis

Michael Goldstein and Sean Geraghty

Something is amiss with American teenagers. More of them are languishing: going through the motions, filling their days, yet somehow disengaged from the texture of their own lives.

This report is about what teenagers do with their time from 3pm to 3am: the hours when school ends and autonomy begins. We think what happens in these hours is enormously consequential for wellbeing, development, and life outcomes. And we think the evidence that something has changed in how teenagers spend them is robust.

Why "3pm to 3am"? Because discretionary time has expanded at both ends. School governs the morning. But teenagers are also falling asleep later: in 2023, only 23% of high school students got eight or more hours of sleep on school nights, down from 32% a decade earlier.9 The window is wider than it used to be. What fills it is consequential.

The public conversation has focused on screens: how much time on phones, whether social media causes depression, whether to ban TikTok. We find the evidence on displacement more compelling than the evidence on direct mental health effects. Teenagers are spending dramatically less time with friends in person, less time reading, less time working, less time playing sports, and less time sleeping. Multiple data sources, using different methodologies, point in the same direction. Whether screens are the cause or merely coincident, these declines are real.

We call this the substitution hypothesis. The path forward may lie in substituting something better in place of screens. As Ross Douthat recently observed, many of us who worry about digital displacement spend our days "perpetually talking, talking, talking … when the necessary thing is to go out into reality and do."0

We take this seriously. The substitution hypothesis is not new. What may be new is taking seriously the implementation question: how do you actually get teenagers doing more? We know, roughly, what activities appear to be good for young people. We know those activities are declining. The harder question is activation: what gets a languishing teenager off the couch and into the world? This report is oriented toward that question.

I. The Data Problem

Much of the academic debate on teen screen time and wellbeing turns on methodological disputes. Researchers are often asking slightly different questions, measuring different things, and reaching conclusions that sound contradictory but may simply reflect different lenses on the same reality. Claims get overstated in popular accounts. Counterclaims get overstated in response. It can be hard to make sense of.

We want to be cautious on both sides: neither dismissing concerns about teen wellbeing nor accepting dramatic claims without scrutiny. Here is what we think we know, what remains contested, and where the evidence is genuinely thin.

Take, for example, the screen question. How much time do teenagers spend on screens, or on social media, or watching TV? It depends who you ask and what exactly they're measuring.

Three answers to slightly different questions
Source Methodology Answer
ATUS (BLS, 2024) "Games and computer for leisure" as primary activity 1.3 hrs1
Gallup (2023) "How much time on social media?" 4.8 hrs2
Common Sense Media (2021) Total screen entertainment (estimated) 8.4 hrs3

These numbers differ by a factor of six. Each measures something slightly different. Yet each is cited authoritatively in policy debates.

The American Time Use Survey (ATUS), conducted by the Bureau of Labor Statistics, is the gold standard for time-use research. It asks people to walk through their previous day minute by minute, recording what they were primarily doing at each moment. This methodology captures focused activity but undercounts multitasking.4 If you're scrolling your phone while watching TV, only one gets recorded.

Surveys like Gallup's ask teens to estimate their total time on social media. We suspect this overcounts: attention feels like time, and background scrolling gets lumped with focused use. (Though we haven't found rigorous research confirming this intuition.)

Neither approach is wrong. They are measuring different things. The gap between them makes it hard to use any single headline number for policy purposes.

CTF's Working Hypothesis

The obsession with screen time hours may itself be a distraction. The more important question is what screens are displacing, and what the cost of that displacement turns out to be.

The effect size problem

Beyond measurement disagreements, there is a deeper issue with the screen-time research: effect sizes. When researchers run experiments asking people to reduce social media use, they typically find effects. But the effects are small, often in the range of 2 to 8 percent of the scale being measured.

Schmidt-Persson et al., 2024 Screen time reduction SDQ behavioral 4%

Effect sizes expressed as percentage of total scale range. A 3% effect on a 1-10 scale equals about +0.3 points.

What does a 3 percent improvement in wellbeing mean? On a standard 1 to 7 scale, it is about 0.2 points. In percentile terms, a 0.05 standard deviation effect moves someone from the 50th percentile to roughly the 52nd. These are statistically significant effects. They are also small enough to be consistent with many explanations, including reverse causation (unhappy people may use more social media) or confounding (something else may cause both).

The one outlier in this table, Hunt et al. (2018), found a larger effect: depression scores dropped from 23 to 14.5 on the Beck Depression Inventory, about 14 percent of the scale. This study is frequently cited, but it is also small (143 participants) and has been criticized on methodological grounds.

We are not arguing that these effects are zero. We are observing that they are modest, that reasonable researchers disagree about their interpretation, and that the research base does not clearly support the strong claims sometimes made in popular accounts.1a Critics like Candice Odgers have noted that "hundreds of researchers have searched for the kind of large effects suggested" by screen-time alarmists, producing "a mix of no, small and mixed associations."

II. What We Know Directionally

Even with messy data, certain trends are clear enough to trust. Multiple sources, using different methodologies, point in the same direction. Something has changed in how teenagers spend the 3pm–3am hours.

Time with friends: the clearest signal

ATUS data shows that Americans aged 15 to 19 now spend about one-third of the time socializing with friends compared to twenty years ago: a 45% decline since 2003.5 In 2024, for the first time in the survey's history, adults over 50 spent slightly more time socializing in person than teenagers did.

The Monitoring the Future survey, which has tracked high school seniors since the 1970s, tells the same story with more texture:

The decline was gradual from the 1970s through 2010: about a point every four years. Then it accelerated sharply. By 2022, 12th graders spent substantially less time on in-person social interaction than their Gen X predecessors.

"Going to the mall has gone down. Driving in the car for fun has gone down. Going to the movies has gone down. We're talking about kids who are spending five, six, seven hours a day on social media." — Jean Twenge, San Diego State University

The timing is notable. The inflection point, 2010 to 2012, coincides with smartphone adoption crossing 50% among teenagers and the rise of Instagram and Snapchat.

Other displacement effects

The decline in socializing is the clearest trend, but not the only one:

Reading: ATUS shows 15 to 19 year olds read for pleasure an average of 9 minutes per day. We cannot say with certainty how much of that decline reflects substitution toward other media, but pleasure reading by all accounts is down substantially. A University of Florida study found daily reading has declined 40% over twenty years, about 3% annually.7 In the late 1970s, 60% of 12th graders read a book or magazine almost daily; by 2016, that figure was 16%. High school teachers report anecdotally, in a growing body of think pieces (most recently in The Atlantic8), that students are struggling with sustained attention.

Sleep: The CDC Youth Risk Behavior Survey found that only 23% of high school students got the recommended 8+ hours on school nights in 2023, down from 32% in 2013.9 Evening phone use is frequently cited as a contributing factor.

Part-time work: Teen labor force participation has declined from 52% in 2000 to 37% today. Some of this reflects economic shifts, but the steepest drops came after 2010.10

Sports participation (boys): While overall youth sports rebounded post-pandemic, regular participation among boys fell from 50% in 2013 to 41% in 2023. Teen participation (ages 13 to 17) dropped another 3% in 2024.11

The directional truth: Regardless of the exact screen time number, teenagers are spending substantially less time with friends in person, less time reading, less time sleeping, less time working, and (for boys) less time in sports than they did fifteen years ago. These are the activities being displaced. This is what we should be measuring.

III. Why Displacement Matters

The debate about teen screen time typically focuses on whether phones are harmful: whether they cause anxiety, depression, or addiction. A different question may be more important: what are teenagers losing when screens crowd out other activities?

The labor market case

Economists have documented a dramatic shift in what the labor market rewards. Research from Harvard's David Deming shows that since 1980, jobs requiring high levels of social skills have grown substantially, while jobs requiring only cognitive skills have shrunk.12

A one standard deviation increase in social skills now boosts full-time employment probability five times more than it did a generation ago, while the effect of cognitive skills has remained flat.13

We hypothesize that non-cognitive skills develop substantially in the 3pm to 3am hours: through part-time jobs, team sports, unstructured socializing, navigating independence, managing commitments, dealing with difficult people, recovering from failures. If those hours are increasingly spent on screens, we should expect the development of non-cognitive skills to suffer, at precisely the moment when the labor market is rewarding them more than ever.

The wellbeing case

Clinical psychology offers a second line of evidence. Behavioral Activation (BA) is one of the most effective evidence-based treatments for depression. The core premise: increased engagement in meaningful activities leads to improved mood.

A 2023 meta-analysis of 22 randomized controlled trials found that individual behavioral activation produced effect sizes of g = 0.85 for depression, comparable to cognitive therapy and antidepressant medication.14 The intervention is "relatively uncomplicated, time-efficient and does not require complex skills from patients or therapists."

Related approaches in positive psychology emphasize a similar insight, sometimes summarized as "do more, feel better." The inverse is also true: decreased activity leads to worse mood, which leads to further withdrawal, creating a vicious cycle. This is precisely the pattern we see in languishing teenagers: not clinically depressed, but inactive, and the inactivity breeds low-grade malaise.

Independence Therapy

Camilo Ortiz, a clinical psychologist at Long Island University, has developed a related approach called "Independence Therapy." Working with clinically anxious children aged 9 to 14, he found that giving them "mega-doses of independence" (assignments to do new things on their own, without parents) reduced anxiety as effectively as traditional exposure therapy.15

As Ortiz explains: "When we do everything for them, we subtract all the uncertainty and then they suck at it. And then they're so anxious because you put them in any situation that has an element of uncertainty and they fall apart."

One patient, a 9-year-old girl afraid to sleep in her own bed, was given assignments to take public transit alone, buy items at the store, and navigate small setbacks. During the final week of treatment, unprompted, she began sleeping in her own bed. She had never done this before.

"Just as anxiety becomes generalized and everything becomes too dangerous and too scary, competence and confidence also generalize. Children's competence gives them confidence, and then the parents get it too, and they're spiraling up instead of spiraling down." — Lenore Skenazy, Let Grow

The developmental case

Jonathan Haidt, in The Anxious Generation, argues that American childhood underwent a fundamental transformation between 2010 and 2015: from "play-based childhood" to "phone-based childhood."

His argument centers on what screens replaced.

"We've had a play-based childhood for literally 200 million years because we're mammals and all mammals play. That's how we wired up our brains. Somewhere in the 1990s, it stopped, and stopped dead by 2010. It faded away and was replaced, very suddenly, by the phone-based childhood between 2010 and 2015."

Play, especially unstructured, unsupervised play with peers, is how children have always developed social skills, risk assessment, emotional regulation, and resilience. The displacement of play by screens represents a disruption to a developmental process that mammals have relied on for hundreds of millions of years.

This is the developmental parallel to the economic and therapeutic arguments: real-world activity builds skills, confidence, and wellbeing. Screens, whatever their direct effects, displace the activities that produce these benefits.

IV. The Substitution Hypothesis

If displacement is the problem, the solution may lie less in reducing screen time directly than in substituting something better in its place.

Why suppression alone may not be enough

Efforts to restrict teen screen use have shown limited success outside of school. China has tried since 2019 with various restrictions, including video game time limits; a Nature Human Behaviour study found "no credible evidence" the tactics reduced gaming.16 Britain's Online Safety Act took effect in July 2025; VPN signups surged by 1,400% within hours.17 Parental controls (screen time limits, content filters) are widely available but rarely effective. The dynamic of a parent peering over a teenager's shoulder asking "Is that really homework?" is difficult to sustain.

School phone bans during the 8am to 3pm hours have strong logic and growing evidence behind them. But from 3pm to 3am, when teens have more autonomy and parents have less control, suppression faces structural limits.

Substitution works inherently. If you are at soccer practice, you are off your phone. If you are working a restaurant shift, you are engaged with real people. Make real life more compelling, and it crowds out passive screen time naturally.

Towards a vision of flourishing

Flourishing is a more holistic concept than happiness. Researchers like Corey Keyes, Tyler VanderWeele at Harvard, and Carol Ryff have done important work conceptualizing what it means.18 But for our purposes, we want to be more operational. We are less interested in flourishing as a psychological state and more interested in the activities that appear to produce it.

Our working definition: flourishing activities are those that involve real-world engagement, skill development, and social connection. They share several features. They happen in physical space, with other people, and require showing up. They involve feedback loops: you try something, see what happens, adjust. They create stakes, however small: a shift to cover, a game to win, a friend expecting you. And they compound over time, building relationships, competencies, and identity.

This definition is intentionally modest. We are claiming that certain activities are worth tracking because they are plausibly good, demonstrably declining, and potentially changeable. The hypothesis is that teenagers who spend more time on these activities will, on average, fare better. Testing that hypothesis is part of the work ahead.

Flourishing teenagers tend to share a common pattern: busy calendars. Their out-of-school hours are filled with a portfolio of activities across multiple domains.

A Week in the Life of Marcus, 16

Marcus plays JV soccer. Practice is Tuesday and Thursday after school, 3:30 to 5:30. Games are Saturday mornings. He works the closing shift at a sandwich shop on Friday and Sunday evenings, usually 5 to 9. On Wednesdays he hangs out at his friend Dev's house; they play video games, but they're in the same room doing it. Monday is usually homework and decompression. He's in bed by 11 most nights, earlier before games.

This is roughly 15 hours of structured activity per week: 6 hours of soccer, 8 hours of work, plus the time with Dev. It's not heroic. It's just a full life. The soccer gives him teammates and a reason to stay in shape. The job gives him money and a sense of capability. Dev gives him friendship. The sleep gives him enough fuel to do it again.

Some teenagers build this portfolio through school activities: sports teams, theater, band, clubs. Others assemble it independently through community organizations, jobs, and informal networks. Both paths work. What counts is total engagement with the world: activities that require showing up, that create small stakes, that build relationships and competencies over time.

We recognize this portrait is easier to describe than to achieve. Many teenagers lack access to these activities, or lack the activation energy to pursue them, or face family circumstances that make a "busy calendar" unrealistic. The languishing teen and the flourishing teen may differ less in character than in circumstance.

Complications

The "flourishing activities" we have described are not equally accessible. The Aspen Institute's State of Play reports document a growing participation gap in youth sports: in 2012, children from homes earning under $25,000 played at a rate 13.6 percentage points lower than those from homes earning $100,000 or more. By 2024, that gap had widened to 20.2 points. Kids from the lowest-income homes now play at half the rate of their wealthiest peers. Youth sports costs have risen 46% since 2019.

This complicates the substitution hypothesis. If the alternative activities are themselves stratified by income, then "substitute something better" risks becoming advice that only works for families who can afford club soccer, music lessons, and summer camps. Any serious agenda must grapple with access.

There is another complication. The flourishing teen we have described (busy calendar, team sports, part-time job, lots of socializing) may reflect one temperament more than others. What about introverts? What about teens whose flourishing looks like deep reading, solitary creative work, or one-on-one friendships rather than group activities?

We do not think the answer is that introverts should become extroverts. Even introverted flourishing requires some real-world activity: the deep reader still needs sleep, the solitary artist still benefits from occasional collaboration, the one-on-one friend still needs that one friend to be physically present sometimes. All teens need some engagement with the physical world. Passive consumption (scrolling without creating, watching without doing) does not serve anyone's temperament well.

V. An Agenda

If this analysis is right, the research and policy agenda should shift. We need less attention to screen time itself and more attention to what screens displace. Here is what that looks like concretely.

1. Understanding how teens actually spend their time

The current measurement landscape is backwards. We have multiple estimates of screen hours (even if they do not agree) but almost no systematic data on the activities that may matter most for flourishing.

We are pursuing two complementary approaches. First, we are synthesizing existing data sources (ATUS, CDC YRBS, Monitoring the Future, Aspen State of Play, Common Sense Media) into a single picture of how American teenagers spend the 3pm to 3am hours, flagging where evidence is strong, where it conflicts, and where we are flying blind.

Second, we are conducting original research: structured time-use surveys with approximately 100 teenagers, followed by 10 to 15 in-depth interviews. The surveys will map activity patterns hour by hour. The interviews will surface what the numbers miss: why teens make the choices they do, what barriers they face, what they wish they were doing instead. The combination of breadth and depth should give us a richer picture than either approach alone.

2. Commenting on the research

The public debate on teen wellbeing is noisy. Claims and counterclaims get amplified. Methodological nuances get lost. We are producing analysis that engages seriously with the evidence, in the spirit of what Jonathan Haidt has done, but with more skepticism toward strong claims on all sides. When a study is overstated, we will say so. When the evidence is genuinely uncertain, we will say that too.

3. Testing activation interventions

We have reasonable evidence that certain activities are associated with flourishing. What we lack is evidence on how to get languishing teenagers to do them. The diagnosis is clearer than the prescription. The hard problem is activation.

Consider the languishing teen: low energy, vague interests, going through the motions. They might benefit from a part-time job, or a sport, or more time with friends. But knowing that does not solve the problem. What actually gets them off the couch? What breaks the inertia?

We are partnering with researchers to test activation interventions adapted from clinical psychology. Behavioral activation (BA) is among the most effective treatments for depression, with effect sizes comparable to cognitive therapy. The mechanism: depression involves withdrawal from valued activities, reducing positive reinforcement, sustaining withdrawal. BA interrupts this cycle by mapping activity patterns, identifying barriers, and systematically scheduling engagement.

Languishing shares this behavioral architecture. Adolescents who are languishing exhibit the withdrawal pattern even without meeting diagnostic thresholds, caught in a low-engagement equilibrium. Our hypothesis: a subclinical population may be responsive to BA-style coaching, especially when paired with practical support to overcome barriers.

The research questions are concrete:

  • Can non-clinical staff (coaches, advisors) deliver activation coaching effectively?
  • What are the primary barriers for languishing teens—financial costs, or the friction of finding activities, signing up, and sustaining participation?
  • Does coaching add value beyond simply removing financial barriers?
  • What do teens and coaches experience as helpful? What barriers prove hardest to address?

4. A flourishing hours framework

We are developing a simple measurement approach: count the weekly hours teens spend in structured activities and developmental settings (organized sports, clubs, employment or volunteering up to 10 hours, arts programs, religious or civic participation, in-person peer time). Both the teen and a parent rate these hours independently.

This dual rating is important. A teen who reports 15 hours of activity while their parent reports 5 has surfaced a gap worth exploring. A teen who reports 3 hours and a parent who also reports 3 has confirmed a baseline. The conversation between the two ratings is often where the useful information lives.

We then correlate these activity hours with two other measures: the teen's self-reported sense of wellbeing, and the parent's perception of the teen's wellbeing. The hypothesis is straightforward: teens with more hours in structured activities will report higher wellbeing, and their parents will too. If that correlation holds, activity hours become a leading indicator—something you can observe and change, rather than waiting for wellbeing to decline.

This is a practical framework for tracking whether teens are engaged with the world, and whether that engagement predicts how they (and their parents) feel about how things are going.

VI. From Languishing to Flourishing

A suburban Boston-area teacher recently described what he called his median student:

Portrait of the Languishing Teen

Heavy screen time after school, over the weekend, and during the summer. Little time outdoors, reading books, walking, engaged in fitness, working or volunteering. A kind of apathy, maybe low-grade anxiety or depression (unclear if clinical). Going through the motions, little direction or ambition. No clear out-of-school passions. Has not found "their people."

This portrait is recognizable to teachers, parents, and teenagers themselves. It captures something that does not fit neatly into clinical categories. These kids are not in crisis. They are languishing.

The screen time debate has been dominated by questions of harm. Harm may be the wrong frame. The question is whether the life that remains after phones take their share is good enough to produce flourishing.

We know, roughly, what flourishing looks like. We know the activities that appear to produce it are declining. What we do not know is how to reverse that decline. How do you activate a languishing teenager? What gets them off the couch and into the world? This is an implementation problem. The diagnosis is reasonably clear. The prescription is not.

The work ahead is to figure out what actually works. Not in theory. In practice. In specific communities, with specific teenagers, through specific programs that can be measured and improved. That is what CTF exists to do.

What CTF is doing

The Center for Teen Flourishing is pursuing four lines of work in 2026.

Understanding teen time use. We are building a synthesis of existing national data sources on how teenagers spend the 3pm to 3am hours. In parallel, we are conducting structured time-use surveys with approximately 100 teens, followed by 10 to 15 in-depth interviews to understand the choices and barriers behind the numbers.

Commenting on the research. The public debate on teen wellbeing is noisy. Claims and counterclaims get amplified. Methodological nuances get lost. We are producing analysis that engages seriously with the evidence, in the spirit of what Jonathan Haidt has done, but with more skepticism toward strong claims on all sides. When a study is overstated, we will say so. When the evidence is genuinely uncertain, we will say that too.

Testing activation interventions. We are partnering with a Boston-area school to pilot activation coaching adapted from behavioral activation therapy. The study will test whether non-clinical staff can effectively deliver this approach, and whether coaching adds value beyond simply removing financial barriers to participation.

Developing a flourishing hours framework. We are piloting a measurement approach where teens and parents independently estimate weekly hours in structured activities, then correlate those estimates with self-reported wellbeing. The instrument will be tested with partner organizations this spring, with normative data collection beginning in fall 2026.

We are further along on diagnosis than prescription. The evidence that certain activities are declining is stronger than the evidence for how to reverse those declines. But we are no longer content to describe the problem.
