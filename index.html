The 3pm to 3am Report

What we know about teenagers' discretionary hours, and what we're trying to find out

January 2026 · Research Synthesis

Michael Goldstein and Sean Geraghty

Everyone has a theory about what's wrong with American teenagers. We're asking a different question: how do we get them to engage more with life? Concretely. The physical world, other people, activities that require showing up.

This report is about what teenagers do from 3pm to 3am: the hours when school ends and autonomy begins. We think what happens in these hours shapes wellbeing and development. And we think the evidence that something has changed is robust, even as the causal explanations remain contested.

Why "3pm to 3am"? Because discretionary time has expanded at both ends. School governs the morning. But teenagers are also staying up later: in 2023, only 23% of high school students got eight or more hours of sleep on school nights, down from 32% a decade earlier.1

The public conversation has focused on screens: how much time on phones, whether social media causes depression, whether to ban TikTok. We find the evidence on displacement more compelling than the evidence on direct mental health effects. Teenagers are spending less time with friends in person, less time reading, less time working, less time playing sports, and less time sleeping. Multiple data sources point in the same direction. Whether screens are the cause or merely coincident, these declines are real.

We call this the substitution hypothesis. If displacement is the problem, the path forward may lie in substituting something better in place of screens. Jonathan Haidt makes a version of this argument in The Anxious Generation: that smartphones have displaced the "play-based childhood" that humans evolved with. We agree with the direction of this claim. Where we differ is in emphasis.

First, we are less certain about the causal link between screens and mental illness; the experimental evidence is more contested than popular accounts suggest. Second, we are focused on a different question. Haidt's work documents the problem. We are trying to figure out the solution. What actually gets a languishing teenager off the couch and into the world? This is not rhetorical. It is an implementation question, and it has been underexplored.

I. The Data Landscape

The research on teen wellbeing is complicated. There are two distinct sources of complexity that tend to get conflated.

The first is the academic literature: peer-reviewed studies on screen time, social media, and mental health. This research is often overstated in popular accounts, and sometimes poorly designed. Effect sizes are small. Causal identification is weak. Reasonable researchers disagree sharply.

The second is the survey data itself: the American Time Use Survey (ATUS), Monitoring the Future, Pew, Gallup, Common Sense Media, the CDC's Youth Risk Behavior Survey. These sources use different methodologies, ask different questions, and produce answers that vary by a factor of six.

Three answers to the same question
Source Methodology Teen screen time
ATUS (BLS, 2024) "Games and computer for leisure" as primary activity 1.3 hrs/day3
Gallup (2023) Self-reported social media time 4.8 hrs/day4
Common Sense (2021) Total screen entertainment (estimated) 8.4 hrs/day5

These numbers differ by a factor of six. Each measures something slightly different. Each is cited authoritatively in policy debates.

ATUS is the gold standard for time-use research: it asks people to walk through their previous day minute by minute, recording what they were primarily doing. This captures focused activity but undercounts multitasking.6 Surveys like Gallup's ask teens to estimate total time on social media, which likely overcounts: attention feels like time, and background scrolling gets lumped with focused use.

Neither approach is wrong. They measure different things. The gap between them makes it hard to use any single number for policy purposes.

The effect size problem

When researchers run experiments asking people to reduce social media use, they typically find effects. But the effects are small, often in the range of 2 to 8 percent of the outcome scale.

What does a 3 percent improvement mean? On a 1 to 7 scale, it is about 0.2 points. Statistically significant, but small enough to be consistent with many explanations. Critics like Candice Odgers have noted that researchers have searched for large effects and found "a mix of no, small and mixed associations."8

That said, small effects at scale can still matter. And the experimental designs have limitations: they typically last only a few weeks, rely on self-reported compliance, and may not capture cumulative effects of years of heavy use. Haidt and his allies argue that the observational data, despite its limitations, points to something real.

Tyler Cowen offers an even sharper dissent. The young people he works with, he argues, are "smarter, more dynamic, probably more productive than any other scientific generation ever, and that's because of social media." He suggests the mental health trends may reflect an "exogenous mood shift" in the Anglosphere rather than social media harm.9

Our read: the experimental evidence shows small effects (more Odgers / Cowen aligned), while the time-use data and parental observation suggest something bigger (more Haidt aligned). Holding both in mind is hard. The displacement trends documented in this report are real and robust. The causal story linking screens specifically to mental illness is weaker than often claimed. Both can be true. And the practical question may not depend on resolving the causal debate: regardless of why teenagers are disengaging, we can ask whether helping them engage more would improve their lives.

II. What We Know

Despite these measurement challenges, certain trends are clear. When multiple sources using different methodologies point in the same direction, that is signal.

Time with friends

ATUS data shows that Americans aged 15 to 19 now spend about one-third of the time socializing with friends compared to twenty years ago.10 In 2024, adults over 50 spent slightly more time socializing in person than teenagers did.

The Monitoring the Future survey tells the same story:

The decline was gradual from the 1970s through 2010, then accelerated sharply. The inflection point coincides with smartphone adoption crossing 50% among teenagers.

Other displacement effects

Reading: 15 to 19 year olds read for pleasure an average of 9 minutes per day. Daily reading has declined 40% over twenty years.12

Sleep: Only 23% of high school students got 8+ hours on school nights in 2023, down from 32% in 2013.1

Part-time work: Teen labor force participation has declined from 52% in 2000 to 37% today.13

Sports (boys): Regular participation fell from 50% in 2013 to 41% in 2023.14

The directional truth: Regardless of the exact screen time number, teenagers are spending less time with friends, reading, sleeping, working, and (for boys) playing sports than they did fifteen years ago. These are the activities being displaced.

III. Why It Matters

The debate about teen screen time typically focuses on whether phones are harmful. A different question may be more important: what are teenagers losing when screens crowd out other activities?

The labor market

Research from Harvard's David Deming shows that since 1980, jobs requiring high levels of social skills have grown substantially, while jobs requiring only cognitive skills have shrunk.15 A 2024 study confirms the pattern continues: the wage premium for social skills has grown across education levels.16

Non-cognitive skills develop substantially in the 3pm to 3am hours: through part-time jobs, team sports, unstructured socializing, navigating independence. If those hours are increasingly spent on screens, we should expect the development of these skills to suffer at precisely the moment when the labor market rewards them most.

Wellbeing

Behavioral Activation (BA) is one of the most effective evidence-based treatments for depression. The core premise: increased engagement in meaningful activities leads to improved mood. A 2023 meta-analysis found effect sizes comparable to cognitive therapy and antidepressant medication.17

The inverse is also true: decreased activity leads to worse mood, which leads to further withdrawal. This vicious cycle shows up in languishing teenagers: not clinically depressed, but inactive, and the inactivity breeds low-grade malaise that makes further activity feel harder.

Camilo Ortiz, a clinical psychologist, has developed "Independence Therapy" for anxious children: giving them assignments to do new things on their own, without parents. The result? Reduced anxiety as effective as traditional exposure therapy.18 As he explains: "When we do everything for them, we subtract all the uncertainty and then they suck at it."

Development

Haidt argues that American childhood underwent a fundamental transformation between 2010 and 2015: from "play-based childhood" to "phone-based childhood."

"We've had a play-based childhood for literally 200 million years because we're mammals and all mammals play. That's how we wired up our brains. Somewhere in the 1990s, it stopped, and stopped dead by 2010."

Play, especially unstructured, unsupervised play with peers, is how children develop social skills, risk assessment, emotional regulation, and resilience. The displacement of play by screens represents a disruption to a process that mammals have relied on for hundreds of millions of years.

IV. Complications

Why suppression may not be enough

Efforts to restrict teen screen use have shown limited success outside of school. China has tried since 2019 with various restrictions; a Nature Human Behaviour study found "no credible evidence" the tactics reduced gaming.19 Britain's Online Safety Act took effect in July 2025; VPN signups surged within hours.20

School phone bans during 8am to 3pm have strong logic and growing evidence. But from 3pm to 3am, when teens have more autonomy and parents have less control, suppression faces structural limits. Substitution works inherently: if you are at soccer practice, you are off your phone. Make real life more compelling, and it crowds out passive screen time naturally.

Access

The "flourishing activities" we have described are not equally accessible. The Aspen Institute reports a growing participation gap in youth sports: children from homes earning under $25,000 now play at half the rate of their wealthiest peers. Youth sports costs have risen 46% since 2019.14

If the alternative activities are themselves stratified by income, then "substitute something better" risks becoming advice that only works for families who can afford it. Any serious agenda must grapple with access.

Temperament

The flourishing teen we have described (busy calendar, team sports, part-time job, lots of socializing) may reflect one temperament more than others. What about introverts? What about teens whose flourishing looks like deep reading, solitary creative work, or one-on-one friendships?

We do not think introverts should become extroverts. But even introverted flourishing requires some real-world activity: the deep reader still needs sleep, the solitary artist still benefits from occasional collaboration. Passive consumption does not serve anyone's temperament well.

V. An Agenda

Useful data on teen behavior already exists: ATUS, Monitoring the Future, CDC Youth Risk Behavior Survey, Aspen's State of Play, Pew, Gallup, Common Sense. The problem is that it is hard to synthesize. Each source has real trade-offs, and reconciling them requires more work than most readers can do.

We think CTF can add value in three places.

1. Commentary

There is a lane for sustained commentary that helps people navigate the evidence without overstating what it shows. Think of it as "honest broker" work: taking the research seriously, telling readers what it actually demonstrates, and flagging where claims outrun the data.

Part of this is synthesis: pulling together existing sources into formats non-specialists can use. Part of it is original analysis: downloading the datasets, merging the files, recoding variables, and seeing what emerges. Jean Twenge does this well. We want to do something similar, with more epistemic caution about causal claims.

This is probably the easiest of our three priorities to get going. Write, publish, build credibility. It may be lower leverage than the other two, but it is foundational.

2. A time-use methodology we trust

The existing data sources were designed to answer different questions. None of them quite do what we want.

Source Strength Limitation
ATUS Gold-standard diary method Primary activity only; misses multitasking
Common Sense Media Rich detail on media use Self-report estimates; likely overcounts
Monitoring the Future Long time series Limited activity categories
Pew / Gallup Large, representative samples Snapshot questions; no diary detail

Can we design an instrument specifically for the 3pm to 3am window that we would actually trust? Can we capture how teenagers spend their discretionary hours with enough fidelity to be useful, in a format that scales?

We will start with a pilot: a small cohort of teenagers, mapping their after-school hours as precisely as we can. We will learn what does not work. Then we iterate. The design includes dual-report: teens and parents independently estimate weekly hours, and we look at where their perceptions diverge. A teen who reports 15 hours of activity while their parent reports 5 has surfaced something worth exploring.

Once we trust the measurement, we can begin coding for flourishing. What counts as flourishing activity (team sports, in-person socializing, part-time work)? What is neutral (homework, commuting)? What detracts (passive screen consumption, social isolation)? The categories are not obvious. But if this works, it could become a product: a "flourishing report card" that families and schools can use. Start small, fail fast, figure it out.

3. Activation research

This has the most leverage but is the hardest to get going. The question: what actually activates languishing teenagers? Not what activities correlate with flourishing, but what interventions cause a disengaged kid to start doing them?

The model: find a site partner (a YMCA, a high school, a rec league), find an academic who wants to evaluate something real, and serve as the interlocutor. Academics are often naive about real-life constraints. Practitioners run programs that never get measured. The lane may be marrying the two.

The hard part is that CTF does not have any kids. We need partners who do. That means relationship building, finding the right site, finding the right researcher, brokering the deal. This is slower work. But it is where the highest-leverage answers live.

VI. From Languishing to Flourishing

A suburban Boston teacher recently described his median student:

Portrait of the Languishing Teen

Heavy screen time after school, over the weekend, and during the summer. Little time outdoors, reading, walking, engaged in fitness, working or volunteering. A kind of apathy, maybe low-grade anxiety or depression. Going through the motions, little direction or ambition. No clear out-of-school passions. Has not found "their people."

This portrait is recognizable to teachers, parents, and teenagers themselves. It captures something that does not fit neatly into clinical categories. These kids are not in crisis. They are languishing.

The question that motivates our work: how do you move a teenager from the right column to the left? Not through lectures about screen time. Not through bans that get circumvented within hours. Through something that actually works.

We do not have the answer yet. We have hypotheses drawn from clinical psychology and labor economics and developmental research. We have a measurement agenda that we think can clarify what is actually happening. And we have plans to test interventions that might help. But we are at the beginning, not the end.

The 3pm to 3am hours are when teenagers have the most freedom and the least structure. The activities that fill those hours shape who they become. For too many teenagers right now, those hours are not being filled with much of anything at all. We think there is more opportunity there than most people realize. The work ahead is to figure out how to unlock it.
