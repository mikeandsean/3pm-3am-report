Center for Teen Flourishing

The 3pm to 3am Report

What we know about teenagers' discretionary hours, and what we're trying to find out

January 2026 · Research Synthesis

Michael Goldstein and Sean Geraghty

Everyone has a theory about what's wrong with American teenagers. We're asking a different question: what would it look like for them to flourish?

This report is about what teenagers do from 3pm to 3am: the hours when school ends and autonomy begins. We think what happens in these hours shapes wellbeing and development. And we think the evidence that something has changed is robust, even as the causal explanations remain contested.

The public conversation has focused on screens: how much time on phones, whether social media causes depression, whether to ban TikTok. We find the evidence on displacement more compelling than the evidence on direct harm. Teenagers are spending less time with friends in person, less time reading, less time working, less time playing sports, and less time sleeping. Multiple data sources point in the same direction. Whether screens are the cause or merely coincident, these declines are real.

But naming what's declining is easier than naming what we want instead. "Less screen time" is not a vision. We need a positive account of what teenage flourishing looks like, and a research agenda oriented toward producing it.

I. Flourishing

The concept of flourishing has roots in Aristotle's eudaimonia, but its modern scientific form comes from researchers who noticed that psychology had become lopsided. The field had developed sophisticated tools for measuring depression, anxiety, and dysfunction, but almost nothing for measuring their opposites. Corey Keyes, a sociologist at Emory, put it sharply: "Health is more than the absence of illness."1

In a landmark 2002 paper, Keyes proposed a "mental health continuum" running from languishing to flourishing. Languishing is not depression. It is emptiness, stagnation, quiet desperation. People who are languishing are not in crisis, but they are not thriving either. They go through the motions. Flourishing, by contrast, involves feeling good and functioning well: positive emotions, engagement, meaning, accomplishment, good relationships.

Keyes's contribution was to show that flourishing and mental illness are not simply opposites on a single scale. You can be depressed and still have moments of meaning. You can be free of diagnosable illness and still be languishing. In his data, only about 17% of American adults were flourishing. The majority were somewhere in the middle, "moderately mentally healthy" but not thriving.2

Tyler VanderWeele, an epidemiologist at Harvard, has built on this work to develop a more comprehensive framework. His Human Flourishing Program measures flourishing across five domains: happiness and life satisfaction, mental and physical health, meaning and purpose, character and virtue, and close social relationships. In a 2017 paper in PNAS, VanderWeele argued that flourishing should be a central aim of social policy, not just an afterthought.3

Barbara Fredrickson's "broaden and build" theory offers a mechanism. Positive emotions, she argues, do not just feel good; they expand our cognitive and behavioral repertoires. Joy makes us want to play. Interest makes us want to explore. Contentment makes us want to savor. Over time, these broadened mindsets build durable resources: skills, relationships, resilience. Negative emotions narrow our options (fight or flight); positive emotions widen them.4

What does this have to do with teenagers and screens? Everything. The displacement we are documenting is not just a loss of hours. It is a loss of the activities through which flourishing develops. When teenagers spend less time with friends, they build fewer relationships. When they spend less time in challenging activities, they develop less resilience. When they spend less time in unstructured play, they miss the experimentation through which identity forms. The hours matter because of what happens in them.

II. The State of the Data

The research on teen wellbeing is a mess. This is not a criticism of researchers; it is a description of the landscape anyone must navigate. There are at least three distinct problems.

The measurement problem

Different surveys produce wildly different numbers for the same phenomenon. How much time do teenagers spend on screens? It depends who you ask.

Three answers to the same question
Source Methodology Teen screen time
ATUS (BLS, 2024) Time diary: "primary activity" 1.3 hrs/day5
Gallup (2023) Self-report estimate 4.8 hrs/day6
Common Sense (2021) Total screen entertainment 8.4 hrs/day7

These numbers differ by a factor of six. Each measures something slightly different. Each is cited authoritatively in policy debates.

The American Time Use Survey (ATUS) is the gold standard for time-use research: respondents walk through their previous day minute by minute, recording their primary activity. This captures focused attention but misses multitasking. If you scroll your phone while watching TV, only one gets recorded. Self-report surveys like Gallup's ask for estimates, which tend to run high: attention feels like time, and background scrolling gets lumped with focused use.

Neither approach is wrong. They measure different things. But the gap between them makes it hard to anchor policy discussions in any single number.

The causation problem

When researchers run experiments asking people to reduce social media use, they typically find effects on wellbeing. But the effects are small—often in the range of 2 to 8 percent of the outcome scale.8

What does a 3 percent improvement mean? On a 1 to 7 scale, it is about 0.2 points. Statistically significant, but modest. Critics like Candice Odgers have noted that researchers have searched for large effects and found "a mix of no, small and mixed associations."9

The experimental designs have limitations: they typically last only a few weeks, rely on self-reported compliance, and may not capture cumulative effects. But the observational data has limitations too: correlation is not causation, and confounders abound. Tyler Cowen has suggested the mental health trends may reflect an "exogenous mood shift" in the Anglosphere rather than social media harm, noting that much of the world appears unaffected.10

Our read: the experimental evidence shows small direct effects of screens on mental health. The observational data on displacement—less time with friends, less sleep, less reading—is more robust. Both can be true. The practical question may not depend on resolving the causal debate: we can ask whether helping teenagers engage more with flourishing activities would improve their lives, regardless of what caused the disengagement.

The synthesis problem

Even if individual studies were clear, synthesizing them is hard. Each data source has trade-offs.

Source Strength Limitation
ATUS Gold-standard diary method Primary activity only; misses multitasking
Common Sense Media Rich detail on media use Self-report estimates; likely overcounts
Monitoring the Future Long time series (since 1976) Limited activity categories
Pew / Gallup Large, representative samples Snapshot questions; no diary detail
CDC YRBS Health behaviors; national scope Biennial; limited time-use data

When a journalist or parent encounters a headline number for "teen screen time" or "social media use," they have no way to evaluate which source it came from or what the number actually means. Part of our agenda is simply to help people navigate this landscape.

III. What We Know

Despite these challenges, certain trends are clear. When multiple sources using different methodologies point in the same direction, that is signal.

Time with friends

ATUS data shows that Americans aged 15 to 19 now spend about one-third as much time socializing with friends as they did twenty years ago.11 In 2024, adults over 50 spent slightly more time socializing in person than teenagers did.

The decline was gradual from the 1970s through 2010, then accelerated. The inflection point coincides with smartphone adoption crossing 50% among teenagers.

Other displacements

Reading: 15 to 19 year olds read for pleasure an average of 9 minutes per day. Daily reading has declined 40% over twenty years.13

Sleep: Only 23% of high school students got 8+ hours on school nights in 2023, down from 32% in 2013.14

Part-time work: Teen labor force participation has declined from 52% in 2000 to 37% today.15

Sports (boys): Regular participation fell from 50% in 2013 to 41% in 2023.16

The pattern: Regardless of the exact screen time number, teenagers are spending less time in the activities through which flourishing develops: relationships, challenging pursuits, rest, work, play.

IV. Action Precedes Mood

The conventional wisdom about motivation runs like this: first you feel motivated, then you act. You wait until you're "in the mood" to exercise, or study, or see friends. Motivation is the spark; action is the result.

The clinical evidence suggests this is backwards. Action precedes mood, not the other way around.

Behavioral Activation (BA) is one of the most effective treatments for depression. Developed in the 1970s and refined since, it is based on a simple insight: depressed people withdraw from activities, and the withdrawal makes the depression worse. The treatment reverses this by systematically scheduling activities and tracking their effects on mood. You do not wait to feel better before acting. You act, and feeling better follows.17

A 2023 meta-analysis of 22 randomized controlled trials found that behavioral activation produced effect sizes of g = 0.85 for depression—comparable to cognitive therapy and antidepressant medication.18 The approach works not by changing how people think, but by changing what they do.

The mechanism is straightforward. Activity generates positive reinforcement: mastery experiences, social connection, pleasure, meaning. Inactivity removes these reinforcers and generates its own negative spiral. The less you do, the worse you feel; the worse you feel, the less you do. Breaking the cycle requires acting before the motivation arrives.

"Motivation is not a prerequisite for action. It is a consequence of it." — Christopher Martell, Behavioral Activation

This has direct implications for languishing teenagers. The languishing teen is not clinically depressed, but they show a similar pattern: withdrawal from activities, low-grade malaise, waiting to feel like doing something before they do it. The wait can last a long time. The intervention that works for depression—scheduling activity, acting before the mood arrives—may work for languishing too.

Camilo Ortiz, a clinical psychologist at Long Island University, has tested a related approach with anxious children. His "Independence Therapy" gives kids assignments to do new things on their own, without parents. The result: reduced anxiety comparable to traditional exposure therapy. "When we do everything for them," Ortiz explains, "we subtract all the uncertainty and then they suck at it."19

The common thread: competence and confidence come from doing, not from feeling ready to do. This is what the 3pm to 3am hours are for. Not entertainment. Not distraction. Engagement with the world in ways that build the resources for flourishing.

V. Why It Matters

The labor market

Research from Harvard's David Deming shows that since 1980, jobs requiring high levels of social skills have grown substantially, while jobs requiring only cognitive skills have shrunk.20 A 2024 study confirms the pattern: the employment premium for social skills has grown fivefold across generations.21

Social skills develop in the 3pm to 3am hours: through part-time jobs, team sports, unstructured socializing, navigating independence. If those hours are increasingly spent on screens, we should expect these skills to suffer at precisely the moment when the labor market rewards them most.

Access

The activities through which flourishing develops are not equally accessible. The Aspen Institute reports a growing participation gap in youth sports: children from homes earning under $25,000 now play at half the rate of their wealthiest peers. Youth sports costs have risen 46% since 2019.16

VI. Our Agenda

We think the Center for Teen Flourishing can add value in three places.

1. Commentary

There is a lane for sustained commentary that helps people navigate the evidence without overstating what it shows: taking the research seriously, telling readers what it demonstrates, and flagging where claims outrun the data.

Part of this is synthesis: pulling together existing data sources into formats non-specialists can use. Part of it is original analysis: downloading the datasets, merging the files, recoding variables, seeing what emerges. This is probably the easiest of our three priorities to get going. Write, publish, build credibility. It may be lower leverage than the other two, but it is foundational.

2. A time-use methodology we trust

The existing data sources were designed to answer different questions. None of them quite do what we want: a granular picture of the 3pm to 3am hours that we can connect to flourishing outcomes.

Can we design an instrument for this window that we would actually trust? Can we capture how teenagers spend their discretionary hours with enough fidelity to be useful, in a format that scales?

We will start with a pilot: a small cohort of teenagers, mapping their after-school hours as precisely as we can. We will learn what does not work. Then we iterate. The design includes dual-report: teens and parents independently estimate weekly hours, and we look at where their perceptions diverge.

Once we trust the measurement, we can begin coding for flourishing. What counts as flourishing activity? What is neutral? What detracts? If this works, it could become a product: a "flourishing report card" that families and schools can use.

3. Activation research

This has the most leverage but is the hardest to get going. The question: what actually activates languishing teenagers? Not what activities correlate with flourishing, but what interventions cause a disengaged kid to start doing them?

The model: find a site partner (a YMCA, a high school, a rec league), find a researcher who wants to evaluate something real, and serve as the interlocutor between them. Academics are often naive about real-life constraints. Practitioners run programs that never get measured. The lane may be marrying the two.

We do not have the answers yet. We have hypotheses drawn from clinical psychology, labor economics, and developmental research. We have a measurement agenda that we think can clarify what is actually happening. And we have plans to test interventions that might help. The 3pm to 3am hours are when teenagers have the most freedom and the least structure. The activities that fill those hours shape who they become. We think there is more opportunity there than most people realize.
