The 3pm to 3am Report

What we know, what we don't, and what we need to find out about teenagers' discretionary hours

January 2026 · Research Synthesis

Michael Goldstein and Sean Geraghty

Everyone has a theory about what's wrong with American teenagers. We're asking a different question, one that seems to get less attention: how do we get them to engage more with life? And we mean that concretely. The physical world, other people, activities that require showing up.

This report is about what teenagers do with their time from 3pm to 3am: the hours when school ends and autonomy begins. We think what happens in these hours shapes wellbeing, development, and life outcomes. And we think the evidence that something has changed in how teenagers spend them is robust, even as the causal explanations remain contested.

Why "3pm to 3am"? Because discretionary time has expanded at both ends. School governs the morning. But teenagers are also falling asleep later: in 2023, only 23% of high school students got eight or more hours of sleep on school nights, down from 32% a decade earlier.1 The window is wider than it used to be.

The public conversation has focused on screens: how much time on phones, whether social media causes depression, whether to ban TikTok. We find the evidence on displacement more compelling than the evidence on direct mental health effects. Teenagers are spending less time with friends in person, less time reading, less time working, less time playing sports, and less time sleeping. Multiple data sources, using different methodologies, point in the same direction. Whether screens are the cause or merely coincident, these declines are real.

We call this the substitution hypothesis. The path forward may lie in substituting something better in place of screens. As Ross Douthat recently observed, many of us who worry about digital displacement spend our days "perpetually talking, talking, talking … when the necessary thing is to go out into reality and do."2

The substitution hypothesis is not new. Jonathan Haidt makes a version of this argument in The Anxious Generation: that smartphones have displaced the "play-based childhood" that humans evolved with. We agree with the direction of this claim. Where we differ is in emphasis. First, we are less certain than Haidt about the causal link between screens and mental illness; the evidence is more contested than popular accounts suggest. Second, and more importantly, we are focused on a different question. Haidt's work documents the problem. We are trying to figure out the solution. What actually gets a languishing teenager off the couch and into the world? This is not a rhetorical question. It is an implementation question, and we think it has been underexplored.

I. The Data Problem

The research on teen wellbeing is complicated. There are two distinct sources of complexity, and they tend to get conflated.

The first is the academic literature: peer-reviewed studies on screen time, social media, and mental health. This research is often overstated in popular accounts, and sometimes poorly designed. Effect sizes are small. Causal identification is weak. Reasonable researchers disagree sharply about what the studies actually show.

The second is the survey data itself: the American Time Use Survey, Monitoring the Future, Pew Research, Gallup, Common Sense Media, the CDC's Youth Risk Behavior Survey. These sources use different methodologies, ask different questions, and produce answers that vary by a factor of six. When someone cites a number for "teen screen time," it matters enormously where that number came from.

Both kinds of complexity are real. We want to engage with both. The academic debates matter, but so do the measurement disagreements in the underlying survey data. Part of our agenda is simply to help readers understand what the various numbers mean and why they differ.

The screen time example

How much time do teenagers spend on screens, or on social media, or watching TV? It depends who you ask and what exactly they are measuring.

Three answers to slightly different questions
Source Methodology Answer
ATUS (BLS, 2024) "Games and computer for leisure" as primary activity 1.3 hrs3
Gallup (2023) "How much time on social media?" 4.8 hrs4
Common Sense Media (2021) Total screen entertainment (estimated) 8.4 hrs5

These numbers differ by a factor of six. Each measures something slightly different. Yet each is cited authoritatively in policy debates.

The American Time Use Survey (ATUS), conducted by the Bureau of Labor Statistics, is the gold standard for time-use research. It asks people to walk through their previous day minute by minute, recording what they were primarily doing at each moment. This methodology captures focused activity but undercounts multitasking.6 If you're scrolling your phone while watching TV, only one gets recorded.

Surveys like Gallup's ask teens to estimate their total time on social media. We suspect this overcounts: attention feels like time, and background scrolling gets lumped with focused use.

Neither approach is wrong. They are measuring different things. The gap between them makes it hard to use any single headline number for policy purposes.

CTF's Working Hypothesis

The obsession with screen time hours may itself be a distraction. The more important question is what screens are displacing, and what the cost of that displacement turns out to be.

The effect size problem

Beyond measurement disagreements, there is a deeper issue with the screen-time research: effect sizes. When researchers run experiments asking people to reduce social media use, they typically find effects. But the effects are small, often in the range of 2 to 8 percent of the scale being measured.

Effect sizes expressed as percentage of total scale range.7

What does a 3 percent improvement in wellbeing mean? On a standard 1 to 7 scale, it is about 0.2 points. These are statistically significant effects. They are also small enough to be consistent with many explanations, including reverse causation or confounding.

We are observing that these effects are modest, that reasonable researchers disagree about their interpretation, and that the research base does not clearly support the strong claims sometimes made in popular accounts.8 Critics like Candice Odgers have noted that researchers have searched for large effects from screens and found "a mix of no, small and mixed associations."

That said, small effects at scale can still matter. A 3 percent improvement in wellbeing across millions of teenagers adds up. And the experimental designs have limitations: they typically last only a few weeks, they rely on self-reported compliance, and they may not capture the cumulative effects of years of heavy use. Haidt and his allies argue that the observational data, despite its limitations, points to something real.

Tyler Cowen, the economist, offers an even sharper dissent. In a contentious interview with Haidt, Cowen argued that the young people he works with through his Emergent Ventures program are "smarter, more dynamic, probably more productive than any other scientific generation ever, and that's because of social media." He suggests that the mental health trends may reflect an "exogenous mood shift" in the Anglosphere rather than social media harm, noting that most of the world appears unaffected.9

We find ourselves between these positions. The displacement trends documented in this report are real and robust across multiple data sources. The causal story linking screens specifically to mental illness is weaker than often claimed. Both things can be true. And the practical question, which is what we care about, may not depend on resolving the causal debate: regardless of why teenagers are spending less time with friends and more time on screens, we can ask whether helping them engage more with real-world activities would improve their lives.

II. What We Know Directionally

Despite these measurement challenges, certain trends are clear enough to trust. When multiple sources, using different methodologies, point in the same direction, that is signal. Something has changed in how teenagers spend the 3pm to 3am hours.

Time with friends: the clearest signal

ATUS data shows that Americans aged 15 to 19 now spend about one-third of the time socializing with friends compared to twenty years ago: a 45% decline since 2003.10 In 2024, for the first time in the survey's history, adults over 50 spent slightly more time socializing in person than teenagers did.

The Monitoring the Future survey, which has tracked high school seniors since the 1970s, tells the same story:

The decline was gradual from the 1970s through 2010: about a point every four years. Then it accelerated sharply. The timing is notable. The inflection point, 2010 to 2012, coincides with smartphone adoption crossing 50% among teenagers and the rise of Instagram and Snapchat.

Other displacement effects

Reading: ATUS shows 15 to 19 year olds read for pleasure an average of 9 minutes per day. A University of Florida study found daily reading has declined 40% over twenty years.12 In the late 1970s, 60% of 12th graders read a book or magazine almost daily; by 2016, that figure was 16%.

Sleep: The CDC Youth Risk Behavior Survey found that only 23% of high school students got the recommended 8+ hours on school nights in 2023, down from 32% in 2013.1

Part-time work: Teen labor force participation has declined from 52% in 2000 to 37% today.13

Sports participation (boys): Regular participation among boys fell from 50% in 2013 to 41% in 2023.14

The directional truth: Regardless of the exact screen time number, teenagers are spending less time with friends in person, less time reading, less time sleeping, less time working, and (for boys) less time in sports than they did fifteen years ago. These are the activities being displaced. This is what we should be measuring.

III. Why Displacement Matters

The debate about teen screen time typically focuses on whether phones are harmful: whether they cause anxiety, depression, or addiction. A different question may be more important: what are teenagers losing when screens crowd out other activities?

The labor market case

Economists have documented a shift in what the labor market rewards. Research from Harvard's David Deming shows that since 1980, jobs requiring high levels of social skills have grown substantially, while jobs requiring only cognitive skills have shrunk.15 A 2024 study by Kim and Kwon, using data from two generations of young workers, confirms the pattern continues: the wage premium for social skills has grown across education levels.16

We hypothesize that non-cognitive skills develop substantially in the 3pm to 3am hours: through part-time jobs, team sports, unstructured socializing, navigating independence, managing commitments, dealing with difficult people, recovering from failures. If those hours are increasingly spent on screens, we should expect the development of non-cognitive skills to suffer, at precisely the moment when the labor market is rewarding them more than ever.

The wellbeing case

Clinical psychology offers a second line of evidence. Behavioral Activation (BA) is one of the most effective evidence-based treatments for depression. The core premise: increased engagement in meaningful activities leads to improved mood. This is not mere correlation. The treatment works by systematically scheduling activities and tracking their effects on mood, creating a positive feedback loop between doing and feeling.

A 2023 meta-analysis of 22 randomized controlled trials found that individual behavioral activation produced effect sizes of g = 0.85 for depression, comparable to cognitive therapy and antidepressant medication.17 The approach is particularly notable because it is simple enough to implement at scale: it does not require extensive therapist training, and it focuses on observable behaviors rather than complex cognitive restructuring.

The inverse relationship is equally important: decreased activity leads to worse mood, which leads to further withdrawal, creating a vicious cycle. Depression researchers call this "behavioral withdrawal." We see a non-clinical version of this pattern in languishing teenagers: not clinically depressed, but inactive, and the inactivity breeds low-grade malaise that makes further activity feel harder. Breaking that cycle, even modestly, may be the key to activation.

Independence Therapy

Camilo Ortiz, a clinical psychologist at Long Island University, has developed a related approach called "Independence Therapy." Working with clinically anxious children aged 9 to 14, he found that giving them "mega-doses of independence" (assignments to do new things on their own, without parents) reduced anxiety as effectively as traditional exposure therapy.18

As Ortiz explains: "When we do everything for them, we subtract all the uncertainty and then they suck at it. And then they're so anxious because you put them in any situation that has an element of uncertainty and they fall apart."

"Just as anxiety becomes generalized and everything becomes too dangerous and too scary, competence and confidence also generalize. Children's competence gives them confidence, and then the parents get it too, and they're spiraling up instead of spiraling down." — Lenore Skenazy, Let Grow

The developmental case

Jonathan Haidt, in The Anxious Generation, argues that American childhood underwent a fundamental transformation between 2010 and 2015: from "play-based childhood" to "phone-based childhood."

His argument centers on what screens replaced.

"We've had a play-based childhood for literally 200 million years because we're mammals and all mammals play. That's how we wired up our brains. Somewhere in the 1990s, it stopped, and stopped dead by 2010. It faded away and was replaced, very suddenly, by the phone-based childhood between 2010 and 2015."

Play, especially unstructured, unsupervised play with peers, is how children have always developed social skills, risk assessment, emotional regulation, and resilience. The displacement of play by screens represents a disruption to a developmental process that mammals have relied on for hundreds of millions of years.

IV. The Substitution Hypothesis

If displacement is the problem, the solution may lie in substituting something better in its place. This reframes the question. Instead of asking "how do we get kids off their phones?", we ask "how do we get kids doing things that matter more?"

The distinction matters because it changes the intervention target. Screen restriction focuses on what to remove. Substitution focuses on what to add. The two approaches are not mutually exclusive, but they have different implications for policy and practice.

Why suppression alone may not be enough

Efforts to restrict teen screen use have shown limited success outside of school. China has tried since 2019 with various restrictions, including video game time limits; a Nature Human Behaviour study found "no credible evidence" the tactics reduced gaming.19 Britain's Online Safety Act took effect in July 2025; VPN signups surged within hours.20

School phone bans during the 8am to 3pm hours have strong logic and growing evidence behind them. But from 3pm to 3am, when teens have more autonomy and parents have less control, suppression faces structural limits.

Substitution works inherently. If you are at soccer practice, you are off your phone. If you are working a restaurant shift, you are engaged with real people. Make real life more compelling, and it crowds out passive screen time naturally.

Towards a vision of flourishing

Our working definition: flourishing activities are those that involve real-world engagement, skill development, and social connection. They share several features. They happen in physical space, with other people, and require showing up. They involve feedback loops: you try something, see what happens, adjust. They create stakes, however small: a shift to cover, a game to win, a friend expecting you. And they compound over time, building relationships, competencies, and identity.

Flourishing teenagers tend to share a common pattern: busy calendars. Their out-of-school hours are filled with a portfolio of activities across multiple domains.

A Week in the Life of Marcus, 16

Marcus plays JV soccer. Practice is Tuesday and Thursday after school, 3:30 to 5:30. Games are Saturday mornings. He works the closing shift at a sandwich shop on Friday and Sunday evenings, usually 5 to 9. On Wednesdays he hangs out at his friend Dev's house; they play video games, but they're in the same room doing it. Monday is usually homework and decompression. He's in bed by 11 most nights, earlier before games.

This is roughly 15 hours of structured activity per week: 6 hours of soccer, 8 hours of work, plus the time with Dev. It's not heroic. It's just a full life.

Some teenagers build this portfolio through school activities: sports teams, theater, band, clubs. Others assemble it independently through community organizations, jobs, and informal networks. Both paths work. What counts is total engagement with the world: activities that require showing up, that create small stakes, that build relationships and competencies over time.

We recognize this portrait is easier to describe than to achieve. Many teenagers lack access to these activities, or lack the activation energy to pursue them, or face family circumstances that make a "busy calendar" unrealistic. The languishing teen and the flourishing teen may differ less in character than in circumstance.

Complications

The "flourishing activities" we have described are not equally accessible. The Aspen Institute's State of Play reports document a growing participation gap in youth sports: in 2012, children from homes earning under $25,000 played at a rate 13.6 percentage points lower than those from homes earning $100,000 or more. By 2024, that gap had widened to 20.2 points. Kids from the lowest-income homes now play at half the rate of their wealthiest peers. Youth sports costs have risen 46% since 2019.

This complicates the substitution hypothesis. If the alternative activities are themselves stratified by income, then "substitute something better" risks becoming advice that only works for families who can afford club soccer, music lessons, and summer camps. Any serious agenda must grapple with access.

There is another complication. The flourishing teen we have described (busy calendar, team sports, part-time job, lots of socializing) may reflect one temperament more than others. What about introverts? What about teens whose flourishing looks like deep reading, solitary creative work, or one-on-one friendships rather than group activities?

We do not think the answer is that introverts should become extroverts. Even introverted flourishing requires some real-world activity: the deep reader still needs sleep, the solitary artist still benefits from occasional collaboration, the one-on-one friend still needs that one friend to be physically present sometimes. Passive consumption (scrolling without creating, watching without doing) does not serve anyone's temperament well.

V. An Agenda

The Center for Teen Flourishing is pursuing a measurement agenda. We are not starting from scratch. Important research already exists: the American Time Use Survey, Monitoring the Future, the CDC's Youth Risk Behavior Survey, the Aspen Institute's State of Play reports, Pew Research, Gallup, Common Sense Media. Researchers like Jean Twenge, Jonathan Haidt, and Candice Odgers have advanced the public conversation significantly, even where they disagree.

What we think is missing is threefold. First, sustained commentary that helps non-specialists navigate the evidence without overstating what it shows. Second, a focused attempt to measure teen time use in the 3pm to 3am window with a methodology we actually trust. Third, a research program aimed at figuring out what activates languishing teenagers, not just documenting that they exist.

1. Commentary on the existing conversation

The public debate on teen wellbeing has become polarized. On one side, researchers like Jonathan Haidt argue that smartphones and social media are causing a mental health crisis. On the other, critics like Candice Odgers point out that the causal evidence is weaker than often claimed. Both sides have legitimate points. The experimental evidence shows small effects; the observational evidence shows strong correlations; and reasonable people disagree about what to make of the gap.

Beyond the academic debates, there is a more basic problem: the survey data itself is confusing. ATUS says one thing, Gallup says another, Common Sense says a third. When a journalist or parent encounters a headline number for "teen screen time," they have no way to evaluate it. We want to help.

We think there is a lane for commentary that takes the evidence seriously without overstating what it shows. When a study is weaker than its headlines suggest, we will say so. When the evidence is genuinely uncertain, we will say that too. When critics make valid methodological points, we will acknowledge them. And when the data does point clearly in a direction, we will say that as well. Mike Goldstein will focus on big-picture framing; Sean Geraghty will dig into the methodological plumbing. Between the two, we hope to produce analysis that readers can actually use.

2. A time-use methodology we trust

The existing data sources were designed to answer different questions. Each has trade-offs.

Source Strength Limitation
ATUS Gold-standard diary method Primary activity only; misses multitasking
Common Sense Media Rich detail on media use Self-report estimates; likely overcounts
Monitoring the Future Long time series; tracks trends Limited activity categories
Pew / Gallup Large samples; nationally representative Snapshot questions; no diary detail

We are interested in whether it is possible to design a time-use instrument specifically for the 3pm to 3am window that we would actually trust. This is a proof-of-concept exercise. Can we capture how teenagers spend their discretionary hours with enough fidelity to be useful, and in a format that scales?

We will start small: structured time-use surveys with a pilot cohort of teenagers, mapping their after-school hours as precisely as we can. We will learn what does not work. Then we will iterate and scale. We will also conduct in-depth interviews to understand the choices and barriers behind the numbers. We will publish the methodology and the data so others can build on it or critique it.

3. Coding for flourishing

Once we trust how we are capturing time use, we can begin to code it. Not all activities are equal. Some plausibly contribute to flourishing (team sports, in-person socializing, part-time work, creative practice). Some are neutral (homework, commuting, meals). Some may detract (passive screen consumption, social isolation). The categories are not always obvious, and there will be judgment calls.

Our aim is to develop a "flourishing hours" framework: a way of scoring a teenager's weekly time allocation that correlates with wellbeing outcomes. We will pilot the instrument with partner organizations this spring. The design includes dual-report: teens and parents independently estimate weekly hours in various activities, and we look at where their perceptions diverge. A teen who reports 15 hours of activity while their parent reports 5 has surfaced something worth exploring.

By fall 2026, we expect to have normative data on what different levels of flourishing hours correspond to in terms of teen and parent perceptions of wellbeing. This gives us a leading indicator we can track.

4. Testing activation interventions

The hardest question is activation. We have reasonable evidence that certain activities are associated with flourishing. What we lack is evidence on how to get languishing teenagers to do them.

Consider the languishing teen: low energy, vague interests, going through the motions. They might benefit from a part-time job, or a sport, or more time with friends. But knowing that does not solve the problem. What actually gets them off the couch? What breaks the inertia?

The clinical literature offers some direction. Behavioral Activation (BA) therapy, one of the most effective treatments for depression, works by systematically increasing engagement in meaningful activities. The approach has been validated in dozens of randomized trials. We are interested in whether similar principles can be adapted for non-clinical populations: teenagers who are languishing but not depressed, disengaged but not disordered.

We plan to test activation coaching adapted from these clinical frameworks. The provisional study design randomizes students into three arms: coaching plus activity stipend, stipend only, and waitlist control. This isolates the coaching mechanism from simple financial access. If the stipend-only group shows similar gains to the coaching group, that tells us access is the bottleneck. If the coaching group outperforms, that tells us activation itself requires intervention.

VI. From Languishing to Flourishing

A suburban Boston-area teacher recently described what he called his median student:

Portrait of the Languishing Teen

Heavy screen time after school, over the weekend, and during the summer. Little time outdoors, reading books, walking, engaged in fitness, working or volunteering. A kind of apathy, maybe low-grade anxiety or depression (unclear if clinical). Going through the motions, little direction or ambition. No clear out-of-school passions. Has not found "their people."

This portrait is recognizable to teachers, parents, and teenagers themselves. It captures something that does not fit neatly into clinical categories. These kids are not in crisis. They are languishing.

The question that motivates our work is simple: how do you move a teenager from the right column to the left? Not through lectures about screen time. Not through bans that get circumvented within hours. Through something that actually works.

We do not have the answer yet. We have hypotheses, drawn from clinical psychology and labor economics and developmental research. We have a measurement agenda that we think can clarify what is actually happening. And we have plans to test interventions that might help. But we are at the beginning, not the end.

What we are confident about is this: the 3pm to 3am hours are when teenagers have the most freedom and the least structure. That combination creates both risk and opportunity. The activities that fill those hours shape who they become. And right now, for too many teenagers, those hours are not being filled with much of anything at all.

We think there is more opportunity in those hours than most people realize. The work ahead is to figure out how to unlock it.
